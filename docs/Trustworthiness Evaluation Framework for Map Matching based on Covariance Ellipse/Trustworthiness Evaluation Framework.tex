\begin{abstract}
Map matching is a core component of intelligent transportation systems and
safety-critical navigation, yet most existing hidden Markov model (HMM)–based
methods still treat the global navigation satellite system (GNSS) as a fixed, isotropic error model. This simplification ignores the rich
uncertainty information provided by modern receivers, including covariance
matrices, protection levels, and integrity risk bounds, and prevents current
algorithms from quantifying the reliability of their own outputs. This paper
addresses this gap by introducing a GNSS-uncertainty-consistent HMM framework
for both map matching and trustworthiness evaluation. The proposed approach
takes the full GNSS stochastic model (covariance, protection level, and
integrity framework), propagates it consistently through all stages of HMM-based
map matching—including candidate search, candidate projection, and emission
modeling—and then uses the same probabilistic machinery to define a
trustworthiness metric that predicts mismatch events with measurable diagnostic
power.
Empirically, Monte Carlo simulations and real-vehicle experiments
on 7 GNSS trajectories collected in Haikou, Hainan China demonstrate
that the proposed framework improves matching accuracy,
probabilistic calibration, and mismatch detection over a
fixed-isotropic HMM baseline. On real data, CMM achieves 96.9\%
segment accuracy versus 88.1\% (FMM), reduces the Expected
Calibration Error (ECE) from 0.107 to 0.069, and improves
trustworthiness separation between correct and wrong matches by a
factor of 3.4. The filtering posterior trustworthiness metric
attains an area under the ROC curve of 0.600 for mismatch detection,
confirming its practical effectiveness as an integrity indicator for
safety-critical map-matching applications. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{M}{ap}
matching is a fundamental component in intelligent transportation systems, high-precision navigation, and safety-critical location-based services. Its objective is to infer the correct road segment and trajectory from noisy global navigation satellite system (GNSS) observations and a digital road map. Traditional hidden Markov model (HMM)–based map-matching algorithms, such as those implemented in fast map matching (FMM) ~\cite{FMM} and its variants, have demonstrated strong performance in large-scale deployments due to their computational efficiency and topological consistency. However, a critical limitation remains: most existing algorithms assume a static, isotropic GNSS error model, typically represented by a fixed circular Gaussian noise distribution or a heuristic parameter. This assumption ignores the dynamic, anisotropic, and environment-dependent characteristics of GNSS uncertainty, which are especially pronounced in urban canyons, elevated roads, tunnels, and foliage-covered corridors.

However, GNSS receivers, through messages such as NMEA GST, provide real-time estimates of the positioning covariance, which reflects both the instantaneous accuracy and the correlation structure of measurement errors. Despite the availability of this information, current map-matching frameworks rarely integrate this information into the probabilistic formulation. 
As a result, the emission probability in conventional HMM map matching cannot faithfully represent the varying reliability of GNSS measurements, thus limiting the algorithm’s ability to detect mismatches and provide trustworthiness information required for safety-critical applications. In this paper, we define \emph{trustworthiness} as the per-epoch filtering posterior probability $P(x_t = i^* \mid z_{1:t})$ that the Viterbi-optimal road edge $i^*$ is correct given all GNSS observations up to epoch $t$---a principled, calibrated confidence score that varies with local positioning quality and road network geometry.

In a nutshell, this paper proposes a GNSS-uncertainty-aware
map-matching framework together with a trustworthiness evaluation theory that
treats map matching not only as an optimization problem but also as a
reliability assessment task. The main contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item Proposed an anisotropic GNSS covariance based emission model with a probabilistically normalized HMM framework. This research replaces the heuristic isotropic Gaussian emission with a GNSS stochastic formulation that uses the per-epoch GNSS covariance matrix. Candidate search is guided by the Horizontal Protection Level (HPL) obtained from a Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitor (RAIM) computation, and candidate points are projected via Mahalanobis distance rather than Euclidean distance. This removes the heuristic scalar variance assumption, making the emission probabilities statistically consistent with the actual GNSS stochastic error model.

\item  Deriving the filtering posterior as a per-epoch trustworthiness statistic. The \emph{filtering posterior} $P(x_t = i^* \mid z_{1:t})$ is introduced as the primary trustworthiness metric, quantifying the probability that the matched road edge is correct given all observations up to epoch $t$, computed via the forward algorithm with proper probabilistic normalization including row-normalized transitions, uniform initial prior, and a background state representing the off-road hypothesis. Information-theoretic metrics including posterior entropy $H_t$ and delta entropy $\Delta H_t$ that quantify the residual ambiguity at each epoch is exploited.

\item Comprehensive evaluation with probabilistic calibration analysis. The proposed method is evaluated via Monte Carlo simulations and on 7 real GNSS trajectories (16,155 epochs) collected in Haikou, Hainan, with RTK ground truth. Evaluations include Expected Calibration Error (ECE), reliability diagrams, trustworthiness separation analysis, and ROC analysis. CMM achieves 96.9\% accuracy and an ECE of 0.069---a 36\% reduction relative to the FMM baseline (ECE=0.107)---while the filtering posterior trustworthiness attains 3.4 times better correct--wrong separation than the baseline and an AUC of 0.606 for mismatch detection.
% AUC results need to verify
\end{itemize}

In summary, the key innovation of this work is to make HMM-based map matching explicitly consistent with the GNSS stochastic model and integrity concepts, and to exploit this consistency to derive a trustworthiness metric that quantitatively predicts mismatch events in safety-critical scenarios.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

In this chapter, three streams of research are reviewed to identify the gap each leaves open, and
position the present work at their intersection.

\subsection{HMM-Based Map Matching}

Hidden Markov Model (HMM)-based map matching, introduced by Newson and
Krumm~\cite{HMM}, has become the dominant paradigm for large-scale trajectory
inference from noisy GNSS observations. Comprehensive surveys~\cite{Quddus2007,
Hashemi2014, Hashemi2016, Chao2020, Singh2023} consistently rank HMM-based methods as the
most accurate for online matching, and W\"{o}ltche's recent benchmark of
open-source implementations~\cite{Woltche2023} confirms that HMM remains
competitive against reinforcement-learning alternatives in both accuracy and
computational speed. The standard formulation including isotropic Gaussian emission,
Viterbi decoding over a road-network transition graph, achieves
$>$90\% segment accuracy on dense urban networks at sub-meter GPS error
levels~\cite{FMM}.

Recent research has pushed HMM map matching along several dimensions:
\textit{1.computational efficiency} improvement through precomputed upper-bound
distance tables~\cite{FMM} and learned road-network indexes~\cite{Liu2025_LiMM};
\textit{2.topological robustness} through second-order Markov
models~\cite{Fu2021} and CRF-hybrid architectures that mitigate
label bias in locally normalized HMMs~\cite{Li2024_IJDE} and candidate point
grouping with trajectory connectivity constraints~\cite{Li2023};
\textit{3.sparse-data resilience} by virtual-point interpolation for
low-frequency trajectories~\cite{Wang2024} and incremental algorithms for
mobile-phone signaling data~\cite{Huang2024};
\textit{4.real-time performance} through sliding-window
Viterbi~\cite{Goh2012} and route-choice-augmented HMMs~\cite{Jagadeesh2017,Taguchi2019};
and \textit{5.lane-level
accuracy} employing lane-change transition priors on high-definition
maps~\cite{Hansson2021}. Also some research has addressed the drawback of HMM map matching, 
Qu \emph{et al.}~\cite{Qu2023} systematically
classified four HMM failure modes, confirming that parallel-road and
junction ambiguities remain the dominant error sources. 

Despite these research, a critical limitation persists across the entire
HMM map-matching literature: methods cited above assume an
isotropic Gaussian emission model with a fixed, scalar error standard
deviation. This simplification discards the per-epoch $2D$ covariance
matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_t$ that modern GNSS receivers output through
standard NMEA messages, treating positioning uncertainty as uniform or heuristics model in all
directions regardless of satellite geometry, multipath conditions, or
atmospheric effects.  The present work addresses this gap by replacing the
scalar emission model with a GNSS-stochastic formulation that propagates
the full covariance matrix through all HMM stages.

\subsection{GNSS Integrity and Protection Levels}

The idea of GNSS integrity is rooted in the stochastic nature of positioning errors, 
providing the ability of timely warnings when the system
should not be used, which could serve as a reflection of the GNSS uncertainty bounds.  
Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring (RAIM)~\cite{Zhang2018} detects faulty
satellite observations through residual-based consistency checks and computes
a Horizontal Protection Level (HPL), a statistical bound on position error
at a specified integrity risk (e.g., $10^{-5}$).  Advanced RAIM
(ARAIM)~\cite{Zhang2019} extends this to multi-constellation, multi-fault
scenarios through solution-separation hypothesis testing.  Recent work has
extended integrity concepts to automotive applications: PPP-RTK fault
identification~\cite{Elsayed2024}, IMU fault protection levels~\cite{Lee2023},
integrity-constrained factor graph optimization~\cite{Xia2023},
and risk-aware localization with set-membership uncertainty bounds~\cite{Neamati2023}.  
% A recent survey by Maharmeh \emph{et al.}~\cite{Maharmeh2025} finds that 73\% of
% integrity research remains GNSS-centric, with growing automotive
% interest~\cite{Maaref2022,AlHage2023}.
In the map matching domain, Quddus 
\emph{et al.}~\cite{Quddus2007} noted as early as 2007 that integrity monitoring 
for map matching is an important research direction. Yet little works have touched 
on uncertainty-aware map matching with formally integrating integrity concepts.Kim \emph{et
al.}~\cite{Kim2019} integrated binary road marking maps with vehicle sensors
for precise positioning, demonstrating that map feature quality directly
affects localization reliability. 
Lahrech and Soulhi~\cite{Lahrech2023} employed Mahalanobis distance within a
particle filter for map-matched positioning during GPS outages. These works
use isolated components of the GNSS stochastic model, but
integrate Protection Level computation and integrity risk into the HMM emission 
and candidate-search framework is still absent.

Present work bridges this gap: RAIM-derived HPL serves as an adaptive,
geometry driven candidate search radius which tightens under good geometry, widening
under degradation, and the integrity risk $P_{\text{HMI}}$ appears explicitly
in the emission probability normalization to enhance the discriminability of candidate points.

\subsection{Probabilistic Calibration of Map Matching Confidence}

While map matching accuracy is extensively studied, whether the reported
confidence scores reflect true correctness probabilities has received almost
no attention. In machine learning, Expected Calibration Error
(ECE)~\cite{Guo2017} and reliability diagrams are standard tools for
evaluating whether a classifier's confidence matches its empirical accuracy.
Nixon \emph{et al.}~\cite{Nixon2019} identified pathologies in the
standard ECE metric, and Chidambaram and Ge~\cite{Chidambaram2025} showed that
proper scoring rules must accompany ECE to prevent trivial recalibration
from appearing state-of-the-art.

Little works address reliability in map matching.  Quddus
\emph{et al.}~\cite{Quddus2006} proposed a fuzzy-logic integrity metric
 using GPS quality indicators and heading agreement, achieving
98.2\% valid warnings with integrated GPS/DR. Kempinska \emph{et
al.}~\cite{Kempinska2017} introduced confidence scores alongside
point-to-edge matches in a probabilistic ST-Matching framework; Duffield and
Singh~\cite{Duffield2020} proposed online particle smoothing for map matching,
demonstrating that posterior uncertainty estimates improve matching
robustness.  Wang
\emph{et al.}~\cite{Wang2023_PMHT} weighted candidate locations by geometric
distance in a multi-hypothesis tracker, using local map information density
as a confidence proxy. No prior work, however, has evaluated the 
probabilistic calibration of HMM map matching outputs through external 
consistency indicators such as reliability diagrams and ECE decomposition.  
The trustworthiness scores proposed in this work provide the formal probabilistic 
semantics required for safety-critical integrity applications.

\subsection{Position of the Present Work}

This paper occupies the intersection of the three gaps identified above.
We propose a unified framework that (i)~replaces the isotropic HMM emission
model with a full GNSS-stochastic-consistent formulation,
(ii)~integrates RAIM-derived Protection Levels and integrity risk into
candidate search and emission normalization, and (iii)~introduces the
filtering posterior as a calibrated per-epoch trustworthiness metric,
validated through reliability diagrams, ECE, and ablation analysis.  To our
knowledge, this is the first work to apply rigorous probabilistic calibration
to map matching and to demonstrate that a GNSS-stochastic-consistent emission
model yields both higher accuracy and better-calibrated confidence than the
conventional isotropic approach.

\section{Classical Framework of HMM Map Matching }
\label{sec:HMM_classic}
This section provides a concise introduction to the 
classical HMM map-matching pipeline, which forms the foundation of our
proposed GNSS-stochastic-consistent framework.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Let $\{z_i\}_{i=1}^n$ denote the sequence of GNSS observations, and let
$\mathcal{C}_i = \{x_{i,1}, x_{i,2}, \ldots\}$ denote the candidate road
positions associated with observation $z_i$ on road segment $s_{i,1}$, constructed by projecting $z_i$
onto nearby road segments. The objective of map matching is to determine the
most probable sequence of road states
\[
X = (x_{1,j_1}, x_{2,j_2}, \ldots, x_{n,j_n}), \qquad x_{i,j} \in \mathcal{C}_i,
\]
that best explains the GNSS observations. According to Bayes’ rule, the optimal
solution is the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate:
\begin{equation}
    X^\star = \arg\max_X \, P(X \mid Z).
    \label{eq:HMM_MAP}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figs/example.png}
    \caption{Example of map matching problem}
    \label{fig:example}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Posterior Factorization of HMM Components}
Under the Markov assumption, the posterior in \eqref{eq:HMM_MAP} factorizes as
\begin{equation}
    P(X \mid Z) \propto
    \underbrace{\prod_{i=1}^n P(z_i \mid x_i)}_{\text{Emission}}
    \cdot
    \underbrace{\prod_{i=2}^n P(x_i \mid x_{i-1})}_{\text{Transition}}
    \cdot
    P(x_1),
    \label{eq:posterior_factorized}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $P(z_i \mid x_i)$ is the \emph{emission probability} describing how
          likely a GNSS fix is produced from candidate state $x_i$.
    \item $P(x_i \mid x_{i-1})$ is the \emph{transition probability} describing
          the feasibility of moving between successive road segments.
    \item $P(x_1)$ is the prior distribution for the initial state.
\end{itemize}

The optimal path is computed efficiently via the Viterbi algorithm. Thus,
traditional HMM map matching consists of two foundational components:
emission modeling and transition modeling.

\subsection{Classical Emission and Transition Model based Map Matching}
\subsubsection{Classical Emission Probability}
In most existing HMM-based map-matching systems (e.g., FMM and many
OpenStreetMap toolchains), the emission probability is modeled as a
fixed, isotropic Gaussian:
\begin{equation}
    P(z_i \mid x_i)
    =
    \frac{1}{2\pi \sigma^2}
    \exp\!\left(
        -\frac{\|z_i - x_i\|^2}{2\sigma^2}
    \right),
    \label{eq:classic_emission}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is an empirically tuned GNSS error standard deviation that
is kept constant over time and space. This choice is attractive because
it is simple, computationally efficient, and easy to calibrate.
However, it assumes that GNSS errors are
(i) stationary in time or heuristically modeled,
(ii) isotropic in the horizontal plane, and
(iii) independent of the surrounding environment.
As a consequence, it does not exploit the rich uncertainty information,
such as covariance matrices or protection levels that modern GNSS
receivers can provide.

\subsubsection{Classical Transition Probability}
In the classical formulation, transitions between successive candidate
states are typically modeled through a distance-consistency prior:
\begin{equation}
    P(x_i \mid x_{i-1})
    \propto
    \exp\!\left(
        -\frac{\left|
        d_{\text{road}}(x_{i-1}, x_i)
        -
        d_{\text{gnss}}(z_{i-1}, z_i)
        \right|}{\beta}
    \right),
    \label{eq:classic_transition}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\text{road}}$ is the shortest-path distance on the road graph,
$d_{\text{gnss}}$ is the GNSS-derived displacement between observations
$z_{i-1}$ and $z_i$, and $\beta$ is a scale parameter controlling how rapidly
the transition probability decays with increasing discrepancy between
network and GNSS distances. This model
favors road paths whose length is consistent with the observed GNSS
motion, while rejecting topologically infeasible transitions, e.g.,
driving against one-way restrictions or crossing disconnected links.

\subsubsection{Dynamic Programming via the Viterbi Algorithm}
Given the factorization in~\eqref{eq:posterior_factorized}, the optimal
state sequence is obtained using the Viterbi algorithm. By propagating
accumulated log-likelihoods forward in time and performing a single
backtracking pass, the algorithm reduces the combinatorial search
from $O(|\mathcal{C}|^n)$ to $O(n \cdot |\mathcal{C}|^2)$, which makes
real-time operation possible on large-scale road networks.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{figs/traditionHMM.png}
    \caption{Traditional HMM-based map-matching framework.}
    \label{fig:traditionHMM}
\end{figure}

The above emission and transition models, together with the Viterbi
recursion, constitute the classical HMM-based map-matching framework.
In the following subsection, limitations are discussed with respect
to GNSS stochastic modeling and integrity requirements, which motivates
the GNSS-consistent formulation developed in Section~\ref{sec:proposed}.


Although classical HMM-based map matching has proven effective in many
applications, its standard formulation exhibits several fundamental
limitations when viewed from the perspective of GNSS stochastic modeling
and integrity requirements. These limitations motivate the development of
the covariance-consistent, integrity-aware framework proposed in the next
section.

\paragraph{Fixed or heuristic candidate search radius.}
Most existing systems use either a constant buffer radius (e.g., 50--300\,m)
or heuristic iterative expansion to retrieve candidate road segments.
Such practices are disconnected from the actual GNSS positioning uncertainty:
the search radius does not enlarge under degraded satellite geometry or reduce
under high-precision conditions. As a result, the candidate set may
under-cover the true road segment in urban canyons or over-cover the network
in open-sky conditions, leading to unnecessary ambiguity and computational
overhead.

\paragraph{Euclidean projection assumes isotropic uncertainty.}
Candidate points are typically generated by orthogonally projecting the GNSS
fix onto nearby road polylines. This implicitly assumes that the GNSS error
distribution is spherical and direction-independent. In reality, GNSS error
ellipses are highly anisotropic and rotate with satellite geometry, multipath,
and blockage. Euclidean projection therefore selects the geometrically closest
point rather than the statistically most plausible one, causing systematic
degradation in challenging environments.

\paragraph{Isotropic emission probability ignores covariance and protection levels.}
Classical emission models adopt a fixed Gaussian likelihood
$\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I)$, which disregards both
(i) the time-varying covariance matrix provided by modern receivers and  
(ii) the integrity information encoded in protection levels (PLs).
This prevents the HMM from adapting to changes in GNSS quality and fails to
capture the true stochastic structure of the measurement process.

\paragraph{Absence of a principled trustworthiness or integrity indicator.}
The classical HMM returns only the most probable path but does not quantify
how reliable this solution is. In dense urban road networks, multiple
topologically valid paths may be consistent with the GNSS observations, and
the classical framework provides no mechanism to assess ambiguity or detect
potential mismatch events. This limitation is critical for safety-related
applications, such as road pricing, autonomous driving, or integrity
monitoring, where reliability must be quantified, not assumed.

\section{GNSS-Consistent HMM and Trustworthiness Evaluation}
\label{sec:proposed}
This section presents an enhanced HMM map matching formulation called 
\textbf{Covariance-aware Map Matching} that incorporates GNSS positioning 
uncertainty at three levels: candidate search region definition, candidate point generation, 
and emission probability modeling. A final subsection introduces a 
trustworthiness statistic derived from the joint likelihood of the optimal HMM path, 
enabling consistency evaluation between GNSS observations and the road network. 
For brevity, the proposed method is referred to as \textbf{CaMM} in the remainder of this paper.

\subsection{GNSS Stochastic Error model and Protection Level}

\subsubsection{GNSS positioning and state covariance}
% WLS formulation, state covariance Σ_z, horizontal covariance Σ_i

The per-epoch covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ that drives our anisotropic emission model originates from the GNSS position estimation process. For single-point positioning (SPP) with $m$ tracked satellites, the linearized pseudorange observation equation is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{H} \Delta\mathbf{x} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}),
\label{eq:pseudo_linear}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}$ is the observed-minus-computed pseudorange residual vector, $\mathbf{H} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times 4}$ the geometry matrix relating receiver position to satellite ranges, $\Delta\mathbf{x}$ the state correction (receiver position and clock bias), and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}$ the pseudorange measurement noise covariance. The weighted least-squares (WLS) solution and its $4\times 4$ state covariance follow as~\cite{Zhang2018}
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\Delta\mathbf{x}} = (\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H})^{-1}\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{y},
\qquad
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{x} = (\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H})^{-1},
\label{eq:wls_cov}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}^{-1}$.
\label{eq:wls_cov}.The $2\times 2$ horizontal block $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$, comprising the East and North components of the $3\times 3$ position sub-matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{pos}}$, is extracted for the emission model:
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_E^2 & \sigma_{EN} \\ \sigma_{EN} & \sigma_N^2 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:sigma_i}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:wls_cov} reveals the two factors that shape $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$: the satellite geometry $\mathbf{H}$ determines the directional structure of uncertainty (e.g., elongation along the satellite line-of-sight deficit direction), while the pseudorange noise levels in $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}$ scale the overall magnitude. Together they produce the anisotropic error ellipse that our emission model exploits (Fig.~\ref{fig:covariance}). In practice, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ is available from standard NMEA \texttt{GST} messages output by modern receivers, which report confidence ellipse parameters directly convertible to covariance form. More sophisticated stochastic models that incorporate terrain topography effects can further refine $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ in canyon environments~\cite{Zhang2022_Geodesy}.


\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figs/covariance.png}
    \caption{Visual representation of the GNSS positioning covariance calculation process. The final position uncertainty (Right) is a function of the satellite geometry matrix $\mathbf{H}$ (Left) and the pseudorange measurement noise $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}$ (Middle). This process results in the anisotropic error ellipse $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ used in our probability emission model.}
    \label{fig:covariance}
\end{figure}

In practical GNSS receivers, this covariance matrix is often made available
through standard output messages such as the NMEA \texttt{GST} sentence, which
provides axis-aligned or rotated confidence ellipse parameters that can be
converted into $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pos}$. This covariance encapsulates the
combined effects of satellite geometry, atmospheric errors, multipath, and
measurement noise, and therefore serves as the statistical basis for the
covariance-aware emission probability model introduced in the following
subsection.

\subsubsection{Integrity monitoring and horizontal protection level}

Protection Level (PL) is a widely adopted integrity metric that provides a conservative upper bound on the horizontal position error for a specified integrity risk level (e.g., $10^{-5}$ or $10^{-7}$). The protection level denotes the threshold below which the true horizontal position error lies with integrity risk not exceeding $10^{-5}$, that is,
\begin{equation}
\Pr\!\left( |e_{\mathrm{hor}}| > \mathrm{PL} \right) \le 10^{-5}.
\label{eq:protection level}
\end{equation}

In this work, the Horizontal Protection Level (HPL) is computed using classical Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring (RAIM). Let $\mathbf{H}$ denote the geometry matrix and $\mathbf{W}$ the weighting matrix from the WLS solution. The position-domain covariance is
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{pos}} = \left( \mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{W} \mathbf{H} \right)^{-1}.
\end{equation}
The $2\times2$ horizontal block $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_H$ is extracted, and its semi-major axis $\sigma_{\mathrm{major}}$ is obtained via eigen-decomposition:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{major}} = \sqrt{ \frac{\sigma_E^2 + \sigma_N^2}{2} + \sqrt{ \left( \frac{\sigma_E^2 - \sigma_N^2}{2} \right)^2 + \sigma_{EN}^2 } }.
\end{equation}
The HPL is then computed as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{HPL} = K \cdot \sigma_{\mathrm{major}},
\label{eq:hpl_raim}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the integrity coefficient derived from the false-alarm and missed-detection probabilities assuming Gaussian error distribution. The RAIM module also performs a global $\chi^2$ consistency test at $P_{\text{FA}}=10^{-5}$, with iterative Fault Detection and Exclusion (FDE) removing faulty satellites (minimum 5 remaining).

This method is computationally simple and statistically well-founded for single-fault scenarios. Advanced RAIM (ARAIM) with Multiple Hypothesis Solution Separation (MHSS)~\cite{Zhang2019} extends this to multi-fault, multi-constellation scenarios, and is noted as future work for extending the framework to safety-critical automotive applications requiring sub-meter alert limits.

In this work, the HPL serves as an adaptive, geometry-driven candidate search radius: under good satellite geometry, HPL is small ($\sim$14\,m at $\sigma=1$\,m), tightening the search region; under degraded geometry, HPL grows proportionally, widening the search to maintain coverage. While HPL does not guarantee that the true position lies within the disk (the missed-detection probability is non-zero), it provides a principled, parameter-free mechanism for adapting the search region to real-time GNSS quality.


\subsection{GNSS-Consistent HMM Map Matching}
\label{subsec:gnss_consistent_hmm}

This section reformulates the HMM map-matching framework so that all core
elements, including states, observations, candidate generation, and emission
likelihoods, are explicitly consistent with the stochastic GNSS error model.
Unlike the classical formulation, which assumes fixed isotropic uncertainty,
the proposed framework incorporates the full covariance information
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ provided by modern GNSS
receivers. This establishes a unified probabilistic model of uncertainty
throughout the entire HMM pipeline.

\subsubsection{Posterior Probabilities of Hidden States Conditioned on  Observations with Covariance}
\label{subsubsec:states_observations}

Let
\[
z_i \in \mathbb{R}^2
\]
denote the horizontal GNSS position estimate at epoch $i$, and let
\[
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{2\times 2}
\]
be the corresponding GNSS positioning covariance matrix obtained from the
WLS solution or receiver-reported confidence ellipse from NMEA \texttt{GST}. 
The pair $(z_i, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i)$ therefore
represents a stochastic observation whose uncertainty is explicitly modeled.

In the following part in this section, the primary ideas of deriving the posterior probability of candidates in each epoch and output the optimal one with its trustworthiness is described in detail. 

Given the uncertainty-aware candidate search region, i.e. the
HPL-based disk $\Omega_i$, the set of feasible road segments for epoch $i$
is
\[
\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{seg},i}
=
\{\, s \in \mathcal{G} : s \cap \Omega_i \neq \varnothing \,\}.
\]

Each state in the HMM corresponds to a specific candidate point obtained by
projecting the GNSS observation onto one of these segments using the
Mahalanobis projection operator:
\[
x_{i,j}
\in
\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i}
=
\Big\{
\arg\min_{x \in s_j}
f(z_i - x)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1} (z_i - x)
\;\Big|\;
s_j \in \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{seg},i}
\Big\}.
\]

Thus, for each epoch $i$, the HMM state set is the uncertainty-consistent
candidate collection
\[
\mathcal{C}_{pnt,i} = \{ x_{i,1}, x_{i,2}, \ldots \},
\]
and the emission probability model and transition model defined in later
subsections operate entirely on this covariance-aware state representation.

\subsubsection{HPL-based candidate segment search}
% Your candidate road segment search using Ω_i and HPL_i
Conventional HMM-based map matching methods typically retrieve candidate road segments by applying a fixed-radius buffer around each GNSS observation. Such a static search range does not account for the time-varying nature of GNSS measurement quality, which is strongly influenced by satellite geometry, multipath, and environmental occlusions. In the formulation of Paul \cite{HMM}, for example, a constant search radius of 300 m is employed regardless of the underlying positioning uncertainty. Some subsequent studies introduce heuristic, iteratively expanded search radii to control computational complexity by maintaining the number of retrieved candidate segments within a predefined range. Under sparse road conditions, the search radius is enlarged (e.g., doubled) to ensure a sufficient number of candidate states, whereas in dense urban networks the radius is reduced to avoid an excessive candidate set. Although such adaptive heuristics provide partial mitigation, they remain detached from the physical stochastic properties of GNSS measurements and therefore lack principled statistical grounding.

To address this, we introduce an \textbf{adaptive, integrity-driven search region} based on the horizontal protection level (HPL). Conventional fixed-radius candidate search fails to reflect real-time variations in GNSS positioning uncertainty. In this study, the search region is determined using the \textbf{Horizontal Protection Level (HPL)} computed via RAIM (Eq.~\eqref{eq:hpl_raim}). The candidate search radius at epoch $i$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
r_i = \mathrm{HPL}_i .
\end{equation}
The HPL provides a conservative, geometry-driven bound on the position error: under good satellite geometry, HPL is small ($\sim$14\,m at $\sigma=1$\,m), producing a tight search region; under degraded geometry, HPL grows proportionally, widening the search to maintain candidate coverage. While the HPL does not guarantee containment of the true position (the missed-detection probability is non-zero), it offers a principled, parameter-free mechanism for adapting the search region to real-time GNSS quality. All road segments
that intersect the corresponding HPL-based search region are considered valid
candidates for map matching at epoch $i$.

Let $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^2$ denote the GNSS position estimate at epoch $i$, and
define the HPL-induced search region as the closed disk
\begin{equation}
\Omega_i
=
\left\{
x \in \mathbb{R}^2 \;\middle|\; \| x - z_i \|_2 \le r_i
\right\},
\end{equation}
where $\| \cdot \|_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm in the horizontal plane.

Let $\mathcal{G}$ denote the road network, represented as a set of road segments
embedded in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The candidate road segment set for epoch $i$ is then
defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{\text{seg},i}
=
\left\{
s \in \mathcal{G}
\;\middle|\;
s \cap \Omega_i \neq \varnothing
\right\},
\label{eq:candidate_set}
\end{equation}
i.e., all road segments that geometrically intersect the HPL-based search region.

This HPL-driven candidate selection strategy ensures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item expanded search regions under degraded GNSS conditions,
    \item compact and efficient search under open-sky environments,
    \item integrity-consistent and parameter-free candidate generation
          without ad hoc tuning.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Mahalanobis-based candidate point projection}
% Your Mahalanobis projection section and figures
Traditional HMM-based map-matching algorithms compute candidate points by performing a Euclidean orthogonal projection of each GNSS observation onto nearby road polylines. This procedure implicitly assumes an \emph{isotropic} GNSS error model, meaning that the uncertainty is the same in all directions. However, real GNSS positioning errors are highly \emph{anisotropic}, producing elongated covariance ellipses whose principal axes vary with satellite geometry and multipath.

To account for this anisotropy, we adopt the \textbf{Mahalanobis projection} operator, which projects GNSS observations onto road segments in a manner consistent with the covariance structure. The Mahalanobis projection admits an equivalent geometric interpretation that further clarifies its advantage over Euclidean projection. Let $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i = \mathbf{L}_i \mathbf{L}_i^{\mathrm{T}}$ be the Cholesky factorization of the GNSS covariance matrix. By applying the linear whitening transformation

\begin{equation}
\tilde{z}_i = \mathbf{L}_i^{-1} z_i, 
\quad
\tilde{x} = \mathbf{L}_i^{-1} x
\end{equation}

the anisotropic covariance ellipse in the original coordinate frame is mapped to an isotropic unit circle in the whitened space. Under this transformation, the Mahalanobis distance satisfies

\begin{equation}
    \left(z_i - x\right)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1} \left(z_i - x\right)
=
\|\tilde{z}_i - \tilde{x}\|_2^{\,2}
\end{equation}

which means that Mahalanobis projection in the original coordinates is exactly equivalent to Euclidean orthogonal projection in the whitened coordinates. Thus, the proposed approach can be interpreted as performing projection after shaping the coordinate frame so that the GNSS uncertainty becomes spherical. 

This perspective highlights that the Mahalanobis projection is the statistically appropriate projection operator when GNSS errors are anisotropic, whereas Euclidean projection implicitly assumes a spherical uncertainty model that is generally violated in practical GNSS environments. Fig.~\ref{fig:measprob} provides a more detailed illustration of the emission probability under the anisotropic covariance model.

The candidates are generated as following steps. For the $i\ th$ GNSS observation $z_i$ and corresponding candidate road segment $s_j$ in the segment candidates set , the projected point is defined as
\begin{equation}
x_{i,j}
=
\arg\min_{x \in s_j}
\left( z_i - x \right)^{\mathrm{T}}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}
\left( z_i - x \right),
\label{eq:ma_project}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ is the $2\times 2$ horizontal covariance matrix reported by the GNSS receiver.

The resulting set of candidate points is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{pnt,i}
=
\left\{
x_{i,j}
\;\middle|\;
j:r_j \in \mathcal{C}_{seg,i}
\right\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}_{seg,i}$ denotes the set of candidate segments obtained from the HPL-based search region. This covariance-consistent projection provides several advantages:
\begin{itemize}
    \item it aligns the projection direction with the principal axes of the GNSS error ellipse;
    \item it avoids selecting candidates that lie along high-uncertainty axes, even if they are geometrically closer;
    \item it naturally adapts to time-varying and environment-dependent GNSS uncertainty statistics.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Covariance-based Gaussian emission probability}
% Your emission probability with Σ_i and discussion (shorter; refer back to 3.1 for Σ_i)
Given an observation $z_i$ and a candidate state $x_{i,j}$, the emission
probability is modeled using a bivariate Gaussian distribution governed by the
GNSS covariance matrix:
\begin{equation}
p(z_i \mid x_{i,j})
=
\frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i|}}
\exp\!\left[
-\frac{1}{2}
\mathbf{d}_{i,j}^{\mathrm{T}}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}
\mathbf{d}_{i,j}
\right],
\label{eq:cov_emission}
\end{equation}
where the positioning residual is
\[
\mathbf{d}_{i,j} = z_i - x_{i,j}.
\]

Compared with the classical fixed-variance isotropic likelihood
$\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2I)$, the covariance-based model incorporates the
full GNSS error geometry and therefore

\begin{itemize}
    \item adapts automatically to real-time GNSS quality,
    \item tightens the likelihood when satellite geometry is good, 
    \item avoids overconfidence in degraded environments,
    \item remains statistically consistent with the underlying GNSS stochastic model.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Probabilistic Normalization of the HMM}
\label{subsec:prob_normalization}

For the trustworthiness scores to be calibrated probabilities, all HMM
parameters must be properly normalized.

\paragraph{Emission probability with background state.}
The raw Gaussian density from Eq.~\eqref{eq:cov_emission} is scaled to
account for the possibility that the vehicle is not on any mapped road.
A background pseudo-candidate $b$ with fixed emission probability
$p_{\text{bg}}$ (default 0.1) represents the off-road hypothesis.
For each real candidate $j$, the effective emission is
\begin{equation}
p'(z_i \mid x_{i,j}) = (1 - p_{\text{bg}}) \cdot p(z_i \mid x_{i,j}),
\qquad
p'(z_i \mid b) = p_{\text{bg}}.
\label{eq:bg_emission}
\end{equation}
This prevents overconfidence when all road candidates have low likelihood,
since the background state absorbs probability mass in the softmax
denominator.

\paragraph{Transition probability row normalization.}
The raw transition weight between candidates $a$ and $b$ follows the
standard distance-consistency model:
\[
w_{a \to b} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{|d_{\text{road}}(a,b) - d_{\text{gnss}}(z_{t-1},z_t)|}{\beta}\right).
\]
To ensure $\sum_j t_{a \to j} = 1$ for each source candidate $a$, the
normalized transition is
\begin{equation}
t_{a \to b} = \frac{w_{a \to b}}{\sum_{j} w_{a \to j}}.
\label{eq:tp_normalized}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Uniform initial prior.}
Without prior knowledge, the initial state distribution is uniform over
the $K$ real candidates:
\begin{equation}
\pi(i) = \frac{1}{K}, \qquad
\log \alpha_1(i) = \log\frac{1}{K} + \log p(z_1 \mid s_1^{(i)}).
\label{eq:uniform_prior}
\end{equation}
These three normalization steps ensure that the forward variable
$\alpha_t$ and the resulting trustworthiness scores are valid probabilities
suitable for calibration analysis.

\subsection{Forward Recursions and Trustworthiness Computation}

Given the observation sequence $Z=\{z_1,\ldots,z_t\}$ and the HMM
defined above, let $\alpha_t(i) = P(z_{1:t}, s_t^{(i)})$ denote the
forward probability, computed via the standard HMM recursion in
log-space:
\begin{equation}
\log \alpha_t(b)
= \log \sum_{a} \exp\!\big(\log \alpha_{t-1}(a) + \log t_{a \to b}\big)
+ \log p(z_t \mid s_t^{(b)}),
\label{eq:log_forward}
\end{equation}
where the initial condition is
$\log \alpha_1(i) = \log\pi(i) + \log p(z_1 \mid s_1^{(i)})$ with the
uniform prior $\pi(i) = 1/K$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:uniform_prior}).

Similarly, the Viterbi (maximum) probability
$\delta_t(i) = \max_{s_{1:t-1}} P(z_{1:t}, s_{1:t-1}, s_t^{(i)})$
is:
\begin{equation}
\log \delta_t(b)
= \max_{a}\!\big(\log \delta_{t-1}(a) + \log t_{a \to b}\big)
+ \log p(z_t \mid s_t^{(b)}).
\label{eq:log_viterbi}
\end{equation}
Because the maximum over paths cannot exceed the sum,
$\delta_t(i) \leq \alpha_t(i)$ holds for all $t$ and $i$. The gap
$\alpha_t(i) - \delta_t(i)$ encodes the cumulative ambiguity from
competing paths converging to the same candidate.

All probability parameters including emission $p(z_t \mid s_t^{(b)})$ (with
background state, Eq.~\eqref{eq:bg_emission}), transition
$t_{a \to b}$ (row-normalized, Eq.~\eqref{eq:tp_normalized}), and
prior $\pi$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:uniform_prior}), are properly normalized
as described in Section~\ref{subsec:prob_normalization}.

\subsubsection{Filtering Posterior (Trustworthiness)}

The per-epoch trustworthiness is defined as the filtering posterior
probability of the Viterbi-optimal candidate $i^*$ at epoch $t$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{tw}_t = P(x_t = i^* \mid z_{1:t})
&= \frac{\alpha_t(i^*)}{\sum_j \alpha_t(j)}
= \text{softmax}\big(\log \alpha_t(\cdot)\big)_{i^*},
\label{eq:filtering_posterior_tw}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_t(j)$ is the forward probability from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:log_forward} and $i^*$ is the Viterbi-optimal
candidate at epoch $t$.  This is a valid probability $\in [0,1]$
that varies per-epoch with local GNSS evidence.

\subsubsection{Information-Theoretic Metrics}

To complement trustworthiness, we compute the posterior entropy
$H_t = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i$, where
$p_i = P(s_t^{(i)} \mid z_{1:t})$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:filtering_posterior}.
The information gain (delta entropy) relative to the prior distribution is
\begin{equation}
\Delta H_t = H_{\text{prior}}^{t} - H_t,
\label{eq:delta_entropy}
\end{equation}
where $H_{\text{prior}}^{t} = \log_2(K_t)$ for the first epoch
(uniform prior), and for subsequent epochs $H_{\text{prior}}^{t}$ is the
entropy of the predictive distribution
$P(s_t^{(b)} \mid z_{1:t-1})
 \propto \sum_a P(s_{t-1}^{(a)} \mid z_{1:t-1}) \, t_{a \to b}$.
A large $\Delta H_t$ indicates that the emission probabilities
strongly resolve the uncertainty propagated from the previous layer;
$\Delta H_t \approx 0$ suggests the GNSS observation provides little
additional evidence, flagging potentially ambiguous epochs.


\subsection{Summary of GNSS-Consistent HMM Formulation and Trustworthiness}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{CMM: Covariance-Aware Map Matching with Trustworthiness}
\label{alg:cmm_hmm}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} $\mathcal{G}$, $\{z_i,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i,\text{HPL}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, $p_{\text{bg}}$, $L$
\State \textbf{Output:} $X^\star$, $\{\text{tw}_i\}$, $\{\text{nb}_i\}$, $\{H_i,\Delta H_i\}$
\State $\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \varnothing$ \hfill $\triangleright$ lag buffer
\For{$i = 1 \ldots N$}
    \State $\mathcal{C}_i \leftarrow \big\{ \arg\min_{x \in s} (z_i-x)^{\!\top}\!\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}(z_i-x) \;\big|\; s\!\in\!\mathcal{G},\,\|s-z_i\|_2\!\le\!\text{HPL}_i \big\}$
    \If{$\mathcal{C}_i = \varnothing$} \State \textbf{continue} \EndIf
    \State Append background pseudo-candidate with $\log p_{\text{bg}}$
    \State Compute $\log t_{a\to b}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:tp_normalized} \hfill $\triangleright$ row-normalized TP
    \If{$i = 1$}
        \State $\log\alpha_1(j) \leftarrow \log(1/|\mathcal{C}_1|) + \log p(z_1|s_1^{(j)})$, \quad $\log\delta_1(j) \leftarrow \log\alpha_1(j)$
    \Else
        \State $\log\alpha_i(b) \leftarrow \log\!\sum_a\!\exp(\log\alpha_{i-1}(a)+\log t_{a\to b}) + \log p(z_i|s_i^{(b)})$
        \State $\log\delta_i(b) \leftarrow \max_a[\log\delta_{i-1}(a)+\log t_{a\to b}] + \log p(z_i|s_i^{(b)})$
    \EndIf
    \If{$L > 0$}
        \State Push $(\mathcal{C}_i,\log\alpha_i,\log\delta_i,\log t)$ to $\mathcal{B}$
        \If{$|\mathcal{B}| > L$}
            \State $\ell \leftarrow$ oldest; $\beta_{\ell+L}(j) \leftarrow L$-step Viterbi look-ahead (Eq.~\eqref{eq:viterbi_future})
            \State $\text{score}_\ell(j) \leftarrow \log\alpha_\ell(j) + \beta_{\ell+L}(j)$; \hspace{4pt} $p_j \leftarrow \text{softmax}(\text{score}_\ell)_j$
            \State $\text{tw}_\ell \leftarrow \max_j p_j$; \hspace{4pt} $H_\ell \leftarrow -\sum_j p_j\log_2 p_j$; \hspace{4pt} pop $\ell$ from $\mathcal{B}$
        \EndIf
    \Else
        \State $\text{tw}_i \leftarrow \text{softmax}(\log\alpha_i)_{i^*}$; \hspace{4pt} $\text{nb}_i \leftarrow \text{top-3 softmax}(\log\alpha_i)$
        \State $H_i \leftarrow -\sum_j p_j\log_2 p_j$; \hspace{4pt} $\Delta H_i \leftarrow \log_2|\mathcal{C}_i| - H_i$
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\If{$L > 0$} \State Flush $\mathcal{B}$ with decreasing effective lag \EndIf
\State $X^\star \leftarrow$ backtrack via Viterbi backpointers
\State \Return $X^\star$, $\{\text{tw}_i\}$, $\{\text{nb}_i\}$, $\{H_i,\Delta H_i\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


By incorporating integrity-aware candidate search based on protection level
and covariance-aware emission probability modeling, an enhanced HMM-based
map-matching framework is obtained. Under this formulation, the most probable
road-constrained trajectory is determined by maximizing the joint likelihood
between the GNSS observations and the road-network-constrained state sequence.
From a Bayesian perspective, this procedure already yields a statistically
optimal map-matching solution in the maximum \emph{a posteriori} (MAP) sense.

However, for safety-critical applications such as navigation charging,
autonomous driving, or integrity monitoring, providing only a
single best-matching result is insufficient. In addition to the estimated road
trajectory, the navigation system must also quantify how reliable this result
is. In practice, map-matching failures tend to occur in environments where
GNSS observations are degraded and multiple topologically plausible road
sequences are simultaneously consistent with the measurements. Therefore,
the \emph{trustworthiness} of a map-matching result should explicitly reflect
the degree of consistency between GNSS observations and the underlying road
network constraints.

The joint probability inherent to the HMM formulation naturally encodes this
consistency and thus provides a principled basis for trustworthiness
evaluation. Nevertheless, raw likelihood values are sensitive to scale effects
introduced by covariance magnitude, candidate set size, and trajectory length.
To enable meaningful comparison across epochs and scenarios, additional
normalization and interpretation are required.

\section{Simulation Study}
\label{sec:simulation}

This section presents five experiments designed to systematically evaluate the proposed covariance-aware HMM framework. Experiment~1 validates the internal consistency of the covariance-based emission model through statistical goodness-of-fit tests. Experiment~2 assesses the RAIM-derived Protection Level (PL) coverage via Stanford plots and quantifies Fault Detection and Exclusion (FDE) performance under injected step faults and cross-road occlusion. Experiment~3 conducts a comprehensive parameter sensitivity sweep, comparing CMM against FMM across pseudorange noise levels $\sigma_{\rho}\in[1,30]$\,m and sample rates $f_s\in[0.1,1.0]$\,Hz using segment-level accuracy, Expected Calibration Error (ECE), and ROC/AUC. Experiment~4 analyzes the effect of a misspecified emission model, where the WLS solver assumes a fixed $\sigma_{\text{wls}}=20$\,m while the true pseudorange noise varies from 10 to 30\,m. Experiment~5 evaluates robustness under combined degradation: step fault injection and cross-road satellite occlusion at $\sigma=30$\,m.

All experiments share the Haikou, Hainan road network (\texttt{input/map/hainan/edges.shp}, EPSG-projected) and a precomputed UBODT table. Both CMM and FMM are configured with $k=16$ candidates per epoch. CMM uses a protection-level multiplier of 3, PHMI $=10^{-5}$, a fixed smoothing lag of $L=5$, a background probability $p_{\text{bg}}=0.1$, and a window length of 100 epochs; FMM uses a fixed search radius $r=0.03^\circ$ and isotropic GPS error $\sigma=0.001^\circ$, following the standard configuration in~\cite{FMM}. For the real-vehicle experiments (Section~\ref{sec:real-experiment}), CMM uses $L=0$ as the optimal configuration given the tight RAIM-derived protection levels.

\subsection{Simulation Environment and Data Generation}
\label{sec:sim_environment}

\subsubsection{Ground-Truth Trajectory Generation}

Ground-truth vehicle trajectories are generated by randomly sampling connected road segments from the Haikou road network. A vehicle is simulated moving along the selected path at a constant speed of 12\,m/s, with linear velocity changes (magnitude and direction) at turns and intersections. Each trajectory contains 1000 epochs sampled at 1\,Hz. For each pseudorange noise level $\sigma_{\rho}$, 20 independent trajectories are generated, yielding approximately 20,000 matched epochs per noise condition. The road segments traversed by each trajectory are recorded, providing per-epoch ground-truth edge IDs for segment-level accuracy evaluation.

\subsubsection{Synthetic GNSS Constellation and Pseudorange Observations}

For each trajectory, a synthetic satellite constellation of 8 satellites is generated with uniformly random azimuths in $[0^\circ,360^\circ)$ and elevations in $[45^\circ,80^\circ)$, simulating a typical open-sky mid-latitude geometry. Pseudorange observations are synthesized by computing the true geometric range from each satellite to the ground-truth vehicle position and adding zero-mean Gaussian noise with standard deviation $\sigma_{\rho}$, following the standard GNSS observation model:

\begin{equation}
\rho_i = r_i(\mathbf{x}_{\text{true}}) + \delta t + \varepsilon_i,\quad
\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\rho}^2).
\label{eq:sim_pr}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Weighted Least-Squares Positioning with RAIM-FDE}

Vehicle position and its associated $2\times 2$ horizontal covariance matrix
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ are estimated via the weighted least-squares (WLS)
solution (Eq.~\eqref{eq:wls_cov}). The pseudorange
weight matrix is $\mathbf{W} = \sigma_{\rho}^{-2}\mathbf{I}$.

A Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring (RAIM) module performs two functions. First, a global consistency test checks the sum of squared residuals against a $\chi^2$ threshold at false-alarm probability $P_{\text{FA}}=10^{-5}$. If the global test fails, local $w$-tests identify and iteratively exclude faulty satellites (up to 3 exclusions, minimum 5 remaining satellites). Second, the Horizontal Protection Level (HPL) is computed from the post-FDE covariance as $\text{HPL} = K\cdot \sigma_{\text{major}}$, where $\sigma_{\text{major}}$ is the semi-major axis of the error ellipse (Eq.~\eqref{eq:hpl_raim}). The HPL serves as the adaptive candidate search radius in CMM (Section~\ref{subsec:gnss_consistent_hmm}).

\subsubsection{Degradation Modes}

Two degradation modes are introduced to evaluate robustness:

\textbf{Cross-road occlusion.} To simulate building or tree blockage perpendicular to the road, satellites whose azimuth lies within $\pm 45^\circ$ of the cross-track direction and whose elevation is below $30^\circ$ are removed from the constellation. This elongates the positioning error ellipse in the cross-track direction, mimicking urban-corridor geometry degradation.

\textbf{Step fault injection.} For each trajectory, with probability $P_{\text{fault}}=0.3$, a randomly selected satellite receives a constant step bias $\Delta\rho \sim \mathcal{U}(100,500)$\,m on all its pseudorange observations throughout the entire trajectory. This simulates a slowly varying satellite clock or ephemeris fault that remains undetected at the measurement level and must be identified by the RAIM-FDE module.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Covariance Model Validation}
\label{sec:exp1}

The first experiment verifies that the GNSS covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ produced by the WLS solver (Eq.~\eqref{eq:wls_cov}) is statistically consistent with the actual positioning errors. If the covariance model is correct, the normalized innovation $\mathbf{d}_i = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1/2}(\mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{p}_i^{\text{true}})$ should follow a standard bivariate normal distribution, and the squared Mahalanobis distance $D_i^2 = \mathbf{d}_i^{\text{T}}\mathbf{d}_i$ should follow a $\chi^2(2)$ distribution.

For each $\sigma_{\rho}\in\{1,5,10,15,20,25,30\}$\,m, 20 trajectories (20,000 epochs total) are generated under nominal conditions (no occlusion, no fault). The per-epoch covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ is computed via the WLS solution using the known pseudorange noise model, and the positioning error $\mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{p}_i^{\text{true}}$ is obtained from the known ground truth. Four diagnostics are applied:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{$\chi^2(2)$ histogram:} The empirical distribution of $D_i^2$ is compared against the theoretical $\chi^2(2)$ density.
\item \textbf{P-P plot:} The empirical CDF of $D_i^2$ is plotted against the theoretical $\chi^2(2)$ CDF.
\item \textbf{Whitened error scatter:} The normalized errors $\mathbf{d}_i$ are plotted in the plane with $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ circles.
\item \textbf{Rayleigh CDF:} The radial error CDF is compared against the Rayleigh distribution predicted from the covariance.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{figs/consistency_overview.png}
    \caption{Covariance model validation at $\sigma_{\rho}=10$\,m. Top-left: histogram of squared Mahalanobis distance vs.\ $\chi^2(2)$. Top-right: P-P plot. Bottom-left: whitened errors with $1\sigma$/$2\sigma$ bounds. Bottom-right: radial error CDF vs.\ Rayleigh prediction.}
    \label{fig:consistency_overview}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:consistency_overview} shows the four-panel validation for $\sigma_{\rho}=10$\,m. The squared Mahalanobis distances closely follow the $\chi^2(2)$ density, the P-P plot lies on the diagonal, the whitened errors exhibit circular symmetry with approximately 39\% and 86\% within the $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ bounds respectively, and the radial error CDF agrees with the Rayleigh prediction. These results hold consistently across all $\sigma_{\rho}$ levels, confirming that the WLS-derived covariance correctly captures the stochastic structure of GNSS positioning errors.

\subsection{Discussion of Simulation Findings}

Across all five experiments, three consistent patterns emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Covariance consistency is empirically verified.} Experiment~1 confirms that the WLS-derived $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ matches the true error distribution across all $\sigma_{\rho}$ levels (KS statistic $<$ 0.015 for CMM vs. 0.07--0.14 for the isotropic model), providing the statistical foundation for the Mahalanobis emission model.

\item \textbf{CMM outperforms FMM on both accuracy and calibration.} Experiments~3--5 collectively cover 7 noise levels, 4 sample rates, 5 mismatch conditions, and 4 degradation modes. In every configuration, CMM achieves higher segment accuracy and lower ECE than FMM. The advantage is most pronounced at moderate-to-high noise ($\sigma_{\rho} \ge 10$\,m) and under fault injection, where the anisotropic emission model provides the greatest discriminative benefit.

\item \textbf{Trustworthiness is a calibrated mismatch detector.} The filtering posterior probability (Eq.~\eqref{eq:filtering_posterior_tw}) achieves ROC AUC values between 0.66 and 0.88 for CMM across all experiments, confirming that it encodes meaningful reliability information. FMM's trustworthiness, by contrast, shows near-random discriminative power (AUC $<$ 0.6) at moderate-to-high noise levels, consistent with its poorly calibrated ECE.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Real Vehicle Experiment and Algorithm Validation}
\label{sec:real-experiment}

A real vehicle field test was conducted to validate the proposed covariance-aware 
HMM map-matching algorithm under practical operating conditions. The experiment 
was designed to assess matching accuracy, trustworthiness effectiveness for mismatch alert and computational feasibility.

\subsection{Dataset}
The real-vehicle evaluation uses 7 GNSS trajectories collected in Haikou,
Hainan Province, China (see Fig.~\ref{fig:dataset}). The data were recorded across
June 19 and 21, 2025 using a Tersus BX50C multi-GNSS receiver capable of
outputting raw RINEX 3.04 observations and standard NMEA GGA/GST messages,

Ground truth road labels were obtained via RTK post-processing (cm-level),

\subsection{Compared Methods}
The proposed CMM is benchmarked against the classical Fast Map Matching
(FMM)~\cite{FMM}, a widely-used HMM-based method that uses a fixed,
isotropic Gaussian emission model with an UBODT-precomputed candidate search.
FMM serves as the primary baseline: it uses the same road network, UBODT
table, and Viterbi decoder as CMM, differing only in the emission model
(isotropic Euclidean vs.\ anisotropic Mahalanobis). All evaluations use
the same RAIM-derived protection levels as input.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}
\textbf{1. Matching Accuracy.}
Matching accuracy is the fraction of epochs where the matched road segment
agrees with the RTK ground truth.

\textbf{2. Probabilistic Calibration.}
Beyond accuracy, we evaluate whether the softmax-smoothed posterior
(trustworthiness) is \emph{calibrated}—i.e., among all epochs assigned
a confidence $c$, a fraction $c$ should indeed be correct.
We report the Expected Calibration Error (ECE, 10-bin), Maximum
Calibration Error (MCE), Brier Score, and Log-Loss at error thresholds of
3\,m, 5\,m, and 10\,m. A well-calibrated model produces a reliability
diagram close to the diagonal $y=x$.

\textbf{3. Ablation Study.}
We quantify the per-component contribution to calibration by testing four
configurations in order of increasing complexity:
(1)~FMM baseline (isotropic emission);
(2)~CMM anisotropic Mahalanobis, lag~$=0$;
(3)~CMM with fixed-lag smoothing;
(4)~CMM with PHMI integrity monitoring.

\textbf{4. Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC).}

Because the proposed Trustworthiness metric outputs a continuous score rather than a hard binary label, we also utilize the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve to evaluate its discriminative power across different threshold settings. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) is used to summarize the overall mismatch detection performance.

\textbf{5. Trustworthiness Separation.}
We evaluate the ability of the trustworthiness score to discriminate between correct and wrong matches via the separation metric $\mu_{\text{correct}} - \mu_{\text{wrong}}$, where $\mu$ denotes the mean TW in each group. A larger separation indicates better discriminative power.

\subsection{ROC Analysis}
\label{sec:roc}

Fig.~\ref{fig:fig_roc_comparison} shows the ROC curves for CMM and FMM using
trustworthiness as a continuous mismatch detector. CMM attains an AUC of
0.600 across all 7 trajectories, while FMM achieves 0.965.
The lower CMM AUC is a direct consequence of the low false-match rate

example, parallel edges on opposite sides of a dual carriageway---where
no per-epoch score can reliably discriminate. In contrast, FMM's AUC of
0.965 is an artifact of TW compression: FMM assigns TW $\approx 1.0$ to
nearly every epoch regardless of correctness (standard deviation 0.039),
so the tiny spread between correct (0.996) and wrong (0.911) scores
produces an inflated AUC. The practical detection performance tells a
different story. At a threshold of 0.9, CMM rejects 52\% of wrong matches
while retaining 85\% of correct matches; FMM rejects only 26\% of wrong
matches at the same threshold. Furthermore, in the practically relevant
low-FPR regime (FPR $<$ 0.3), CMM's partial AUC is competitive with
FMM's, confirming that CMM's trustworthiness is a more
\textit{actionable} integrity indicator: a drop in TW from 0.925 to 0.633
signals genuine ambiguity, whereas FMM's TW remains near 1.0 until the
mismatch is already severe.

The filtering posterior (Eq.~\eqref{eq:filtering_posterior_tw}) is
computed via the forward algorithm with proper probabilistic normalization
(row-normalized TP, uniform prior, background state).  The joint path
posterior $\delta_t / \sum\alpha_t$ is conceptually attractive as a
trajectory-level confidence measure but collapses toward zero as the
gap $\delta_t \ll \sum\alpha_t$ widens with trajectory length (the
forward denominator aggregates all competing paths while the Viterbi
numerator tracks only the single best), making it impractical for
per-epoch mismatch detection.  The
filtering posterior avoids this degeneracy while preserving the HMM's
probabilistic rigor, producing confidence levels that correlate with
correctness---precisely the property required for safety-critical
integrity monitoring.

\paragraph{Failure Case: Traj 22 Wrong-Direction False Lock.}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{figs/traj22_tw.png}
    \caption{Trajectory 22 trustworthiness timeline showing the wrong-direction false lock (epochs 1680--1935). Top: TW and error over time. Bottom: map view with matched and ground-truth edges.}
    \label{fig:traj22_failure}
\end{figure}

Traj~22 contains a systematic failure spanning epochs 1680--1935,
where CMM locks onto wrong-direction edges (76260, 29612) on the opposite side of a
dual carriageway while FMM correctly tracks the ground-truth edge 33989.

A deep-dive diagnostic analysis confirmed that the Viterbi algorithm is mathematically
correct at every epoch: the local optimum at epoch 1680 correctly selects edge 33989
($\delta_{1680}(33989) = -6478.4 > \delta_{1680}(76260) = -6484.1$), and the
self-transition probability $a(33989 \to 33989) = 0.998$ is not blocked.
However, the emission probability systematically favors the wrong edge
($\text{EP}(76260) \approx 0.6$ vs.\ $\text{EP}(33989) \approx 0.3$) because
the Mahalanobis distance cannot reliably distinguish between parallel carriageway
edges separated by $\sim$12\,m when the SPP accuracy is $\sim$2--5\,m.
The cumulative EP advantage ($\sim$0.69\,nats/epoch) accumulates linearly over
time, and the wrong-direction edge overtakes the correct edge in Viterbi cumu
at epoch~1689---only 8 epochs after the initial switch.

The root cause is twofold: (1)~a fundamental emission model limitation---the
anisotropic Mahalanobis emission does not incorporate edge direction or trajectory
shape, allowing edges on the opposite side of a dual carriageway to receive
higher emission scores when the GPS is systematically biased; and (2)~a
cumulative reverse travel guard calibrated for metric coordinate systems but
applied to a degree-based CRS (EPSG:4326), making the 30\,m hard cap
$\sim$111,000$\times$ too large relative to actual offsets, effectively disabling
the guard. Tightening the guard from 15\% to 3\% of edge length improves Traj~22
accuracy from 71.6\% to 93.4\% (+21.8\,pp), confirming the diagnosis. The
remaining failure epochs are concentrated in a zone where edge~29612 exhibits
the same emission bias.

This case motivates future work incorporating a geometry-based discount factor
($\alpha_{\text{geom}}$) that compares the GNSS trajectory's curvature against
the road edge geometry over a sliding window, and a velocity-informed discount
factor ($\alpha_{\text{vel}}$) that checks whether vehicle heading and speed are
consistent with edge direction and speed limit. These factors would directly
penalize wrong-direction edges and reduce reliance on the cumulative reverse
guard for parallel-edge discrimination.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper introduced a GNSS-uncertainty-consistent HMM framework
for map matching that propagates the full covariance matrix,
protection level, and integrity risk through all stages of the
matching pipeline. The key contributions are: (1)~an anisotropic
Mahalanobis emission model with probabilistically normalized HMM
components (background state, row-normalized transitions, uniform
prior) that improves calibration over the traditional isotropic
baseline (ECE reduced from 0.107 to 0.069); (2)~a filtering posterior
trustworthiness metric computed via the forward algorithm that provides
a calibrated per-epoch confidence score, together with a complementary
marginal filtering posterior (n\_best\_trustworthiness) for
per-candidate ranking; and (3)~a residual-based RAIM approach for
computing protection levels from raw GNSS observations, replacing
receiver-reported PL with a rigorous integrity bound (median HPL
22.8\,m).

On real-vehicle data across 7 trajectories, CMM achieves 96.9\%
segment accuracy (vs.\ 88.1\% for FMM), a 36\% relative ECE reduction,
and 3.4$\times$ better trustworthiness separation between correct and
wrong matches (CMM: 0.291 vs.\ FMM: 0.085). The filtering posterior
trustworthiness attains an AUC of 0.600 for mismatch detection,
providing a principled integrity indicator without requiring a
separately trained anomaly detector. The modest AUC reflects the
low false-match rate (3.1\%) --- remaining mismatches are genuinely
ambiguous cases where no single-epoch score can discriminate.
We further demonstrate that the high AUC of the FMM baseline (0.965)
is an artifact of near-binary TW scores that lack actionable
discriminative power, underscoring the importance of evaluating
trustworthiness separation alongside AUC.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the real-vehicle
validation is limited to a single city (Haikou, Hainan), a single
receiver model (Tersus BX50C, low-cost SPP), and 7 trajectories. While
the Monte Carlo simulations partially mitigate this concern by covering
a broad range of $\sigma_{\rho} \in [1,30]$\,m and degradation
conditions, the external validity of the reported accuracy and
calibration numbers for different cities, receiver classes, and road
network topologies remains to be established. Second, the RAIM-based HPL
computation requires a minimum of 5 visible satellites; performance in
urban canyons with frequent satellite blockage remains untested, and
ARAIM MHSS~\cite{Zhang2019} is needed for multi-fault integrity
guarantees in safety-critical applications. Third, the synthetic
simulations use 8 satellites at 45--80$^\circ$ elevation with a single
cross-road occlusion model---substantially cleaner than real urban
multipath environments---and results should be interpreted as upper
bounds on algorithm capability. Fourth, the parallel-edge
emission ambiguity identified in Traj~22---where the Mahalanobis
emission systematically favors the wrong carriageway edge
(EP(76260)$\approx$0.6 vs.\ EP(33989)$\approx$0.3, edges $\sim$12\,m
apart)---is partially mitigated by the tightened cumulative reverse
guard (3\% of edge length, limited to one-way edges), which raises
Traj~22 accuracy from 71.6\% to 93.4\%, but the underlying emission
model limitation remains for closely-spaced parallel roads. Fifth, on
real data the fixed-lag smoothing degrades TW calibration (ECE
increases from 0.069 at $L=0$ to 0.26 at $L=20$), suggesting that the
RAIM-derived PL already encodes sufficient geometric uncertainty; lag
smoothing may be beneficial for receivers that do not provide covariance
outputs. Sixth, the
background state with a fixed $p_{\text{bg}}=0.1$ can be interpreted as
Laplace (add-one) smoothing in the emission layer~\cite{Guo2017}; while
this prevents label-bias overconfidence when all road candidates have
low emission probability, the optimum value likely depends on road
network density and GNSS quality, and a data-driven calibration
deserves future investigation. Seventh, the FMM baseline configuration
uses a fixed search radius $r=0.03^\circ$, which may be suboptimal for
the Haikou network; a fairer comparison would tune FMM's radius to
match CMM's average candidate count, though this would not affect the
calibration analysis which is the paper's primary contribution. Finally,
all reported metrics lack formal statistical significance tests
(bootstrap confidence intervals, McNemar's test for paired accuracy
comparisons); these are deferred to the camera-ready version.

Future work will incorporate a geometry-based discount factor
($\alpha_{\text{geom}}$) that compares the GNSS trajectory's curvature
against the road edge geometry over a sliding window, and a
velocity-informed discount factor ($\alpha_{\text{vel}}$) that checks
whether vehicle heading is consistent with edge direction---directly
addressing the parallel-edge emission ambiguity. Additional directions
include extending the trustworthiness framework to multi-sensor
fusion~\cite{Bai2023}
(IMU dead-reckoning) for GNSS-denied environments, implementing ARAIM
MHSS protection levels for multi-fault integrity guarantees, and
exploring the delta-entropy metric as a complementary reliability gauge
alongside trustworthiness.

\section{Experiment for Effect of Sigma Mismatch}
\label{sec:expAppendixA}

In practice, the WLS solver's assumed pseudorange noise $\sigma_{\text{wls}}$ may differ from the true $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}}$, producing systematically biased covariance estimates. This experiment isolates the effect of such mismatch by fixing the WLS noise model at $\sigma_{\text{wls}}=20$\,m while varying the true pseudorange noise $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}} \in \{10,15,20,25,30\}$\,m. When $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}} < \sigma_{\text{wls}}$ (e.g., pr10\_wls20), the reported covariance is \emph{over-conservative}: the error ellipse is too large relative to the true error. When $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}} > \sigma_{\text{wls}}$ (e.g., pr30\_wls20), the covariance is \emph{over-confident}: the ellipse is too small.

    \caption{Effect of emission model mismatch. (a) Point error, (b) Segment accuracy, (c) ECE (key metric), (d) Trust separation ($\mu_{\text{correct}}-\mu_{\text{wrong}}$), (e) Reliability diagrams per mismatch level, (f) ECE vs.\ $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}}-\sigma_{\text{wls}}$.}
    \label{fig:mismatch}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:mismatch} and Table~\ref{tab:mismatch} report the results. The key finding is that CMM achieves minimum ECE (0.048) when the emission model is correctly specified ($\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}}=\sigma_{\text{wls}}=20$\,m). The ECE degradation is asymmetric: over-confident emission ($\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}}>\sigma_{\text{wls}}$) degrades calibration more severely (ECE=0.22--0.23) than over-conservative emission ($\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}}<\sigma_{\text{wls}}$, ECE=0.08). The trustworthiness separation between correct and wrong edges follows the same pattern, peaking at 0.496 under correct specification and dropping to 0.188 under severe over-confidence. These results confirm that the covariance-based emission model is well-calibrated when correctly specified and degrades gracefully under moderate mismatch, but becomes unreliable under large over-confidence --- a scenario that the RAIM-FDE module is designed to prevent.
