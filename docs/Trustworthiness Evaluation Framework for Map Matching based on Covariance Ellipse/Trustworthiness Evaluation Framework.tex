\begin{abstract}
Map matching is a core component of intelligent transportation systems and
safety-critical navigation, yet most existing hidden Markov model (HMM)–based
methods still treat the global navigation satellite system (GNSS) as a fixed, isotropic error model. This simplification ignores the rich
uncertainty information provided by modern receivers, including covariance
matrices, protection levels, and integrity risk bounds, and prevents current
algorithms from quantifying the reliability of their own outputs. This paper
addresses this gap by introducing a GNSS-uncertainty-consistent HMM framework
for both map matching and trustworthiness evaluation. The proposed approach
takes the full GNSS stochastic model (covariance, protection level, and
integrity framework), propagates it consistently through all stages of HMM-based
map matching—including candidate search, candidate projection, and emission
modeling—and then uses the same probabilistic machinery to define a
trustworthiness metric that predicts mismatch events with measurable diagnostic
power.
Empirically, Monte Carlo simulations and real-vehicle experiments
on 7 GNSS trajectories collected in Haikou, Hainan China demonstrate
that the proposed framework improves matching accuracy,
probabilistic calibration, and mismatch detection over a
fixed-isotropic HMM baseline. On real data, CMM achieves 91.4\%
segment accuracy versus 88.1\% (FMM), reduces the Expected
Calibration Error (ECE) from 0.107 to 0.078, and improves
trustworthiness separation between correct and wrong matches by a
factor of 3.9. The joint path posterior trustworthiness metric
attains an area under the ROC curve of 0.764 for mismatch detection,
confirming its practical effectiveness as an integrity indicator for
safety-critical map-matching applications.
\end{abstract}

Its objective is to infer the correct road segment and trajectory from noisy global navigation satellite system (GNSS) observations and a digital road map. Traditional hidden Markov model (HMM)–based map-matching algorithms, such as those implemented in fast map matching (FMM) and its variants, have demonstrated strong performance in large-scale deployments due to their computational efficiency and topological consistency. To overcome these limitations, this paper proposes a GNSS-uncertainty-aware
map-matching framework together with a trustworthiness evaluation theory that
treats map matching not only as a optimization problem but also as a
reliability assessment task. The main contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item Proposed an anisotropic GNSS covariance based emission model with a probabilistically normalized HMM framework. This research replaces the standard isotropic Gaussian emission with a GNSS stochastic formulation that uses the per-epoch GNSS covariance matrix, introduces a background state for emission probability normalization, enforces row-normalized transition probabilities, and adopts a uniform prior over candidates. Candidate search is guided by the Horizontal Protection Level (HPL) obtained from a Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitor (RAIM) computation, and candidate points are projected via Mahalanobis distance rather than Euclidean distance. This removes the heuristic scalar variance assumption, making the emission probabilities statistically consistent with the actual GNSS stochastic error model.
% TW formula updated to joint path posterior
\item  Deriving joint path posterior and marginal filtering posterior as complementary trustworthiness statistics. Two trustworthiness metrics are introduced: the \emph{joint path posterior} $P(H^*_{1:t} \mid z_{1:t})$, which quantifies the probability that the entire optimal partial path up to epoch $t$ is correct, and the \emph{marginal filtering posterior} $P(s_t^{(i)} \mid z_{1:t})$, which captures per-candidate uncertainty at each epoch. These are complemented by information-theoretic metrics (posterior entropy $H_t$ and delta entropy $\Delta H_t$) that quantify the residual ambiguity at each epoch.

\item Comprehensive evaluation with probabilistic calibration analysis. The proposed method is evaluated via Monte Carlo simulations and on 7 real GNSS trajectories (16,155 epochs) collected in Haikou, Hainan, with RTK ground truth. Evaluations include Expected Calibration Error (ECE), reliability diagrams, trustworthiness separation analysis, and ROC analysis. CMM achieves 91.4\% accuracy and an ECE of 0.078---reducing error by 27\% relative to the FMM baseline---while the joint path posterior trustworthiness attains an AUC of \textbf{0.764} for mismatch detection with 3.9 times better correct--wrong separation than the baseline.

\end{itemize}

In summary, the key innovation of this work is to make HMM-based map matching explicitly consistent with the GNSS stochastic model and integrity concepts, and to exploit this consistency to derive a trustworthiness metric that both improves matching performance and quantitatively predicts mismatch events in safety-critical scenarios.

\subsection{HMM-Based Map Matching}

Since the work of Newson and Krumm~\cite{HMM}, Hidden Markov
Model (HMM)-based map matching has become the dominant paradigm for
inferring vehicle trajectories from noisy GNSS observations. The
standard formulation treats each GNSS point as an observation and
candidate road positions as hidden states, where Viterbi algorithm is used
to find the most likely state sequence. Comprehensive surveys by
Quddus \emph{et al.}~\cite{Quddus2007}, Hashemi and
Karimi~\cite{Hashemi2014}, Chao \emph{et al.}~\cite{Chao2020}, and
Singh \emph{et al.}~\cite{Singh2023} have systematically categorized
map-matching algorithms into geometric, topological, probabilistic,
and advanced (Kalman filter, fuzzy logic, belief theory) classes, with
HMM-based methods consistently achieving the highest accuracy for
online matching tasks. W\"{o}ltche~\cite{Woltche2023} provided a
detailed benchmark of open-source HMM implementations alongside a
novel Markov Decision Process formulation with reinforcement learning,
demonstrating that HMM-based methods remain competitive in both accuracy
and computational speed.

performance. Liu \emph{et al.}~\cite{Liu2025_LiMM} further
accelerated HMM Viterbi decoding by replacing hand-crafted index
structures with learned road-network indexes, achieving an
11.7$\times$ speedup with only 1.8\% accuracy loss. Jagadeesh and
Srikanthan~\cite{Jagadeesh2017}
incorporated route choice models into online HMM map matching to

route prediction model trained on historical trajectory data to reduce
online matching latency. Wang \emph{et al.}~\cite{Wang2024} extended
HMM map matching to low-frequency trajectories through probability
interpolation of virtual points. Li \emph{et al.}~\cite{Li2023}
improved emission modeling by accounting for road network density
variations. Li \emph{et al.}~\cite{Li2024_IJDE} proposed an HMM-CRF
(Conditional Random Field) hybrid model that solves the labeling bias
problem inherent in local normalization of HMM.

\subsection{GNSS Integrity and Protection Levels}

GNSS integrity, the ability to provide timely warnings when the system
should not be used, originated in safety-critical aviation
applications. Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring
(RAIM)~\cite{Zhang2018} detects faulty satellite observations through
residual-based consistency checks. Advanced RAIM (ARAIM)~\cite{Zhang2019} extends this framework to
multi-constellation, dual-frequency scenarios, computing horizontal
and vertical protection levels (HPL/VPL) through solution-separation
hypothesis testing. A recent comprehensive survey by Maharmeh \emph{et
al.}~\cite{Maharmeh2024} classifies integrity methods into three
categories: Fault Detection and Exclusion (FDE), Protection Level (PL)
computation, and Robust Modeling, with 73.3\% of published
integrity research remains GNSS-centric.

developed a RAIM framework fusing cellular Signals of Opportunity (SOPs)
with IMU data for GNSS-denied vehicular navigation, achieving 66\%
RMSE reduction through measurement exclusion. Al Hage \emph{et

high-integrity vehicle localization using GPS, Galileo, camera, and HD
map measurements. Elsayed \emph{et al.}~\cite{Elsayed2024} addressed
computational efficiency in PPP-RTK integrity monitoring through a
user-end UDUC-residual ratio method, achieving 85--99\% reduction in
FDE computational load. Xia \emph{et al.}~\cite{Xia2023} integrated
switchable constraints into factor graph optimization for
integrity-constrained GNSS positioning in urban canyons.

\subsection{Probabilistic Calibration and Trustworthiness}

While map matching accuracy (percentage of correctly identified road
segments) is extensively studied, whether confidence scores reflect true correctness has received little attention in the map matching literature.
In machine learning, Expected Calibration Error (ECE)~\cite{Guo2017}
and reliability diagrams % what os reliability diagrams, need to add
are standard tools for evaluating
probabilistic classifier calibration. Nixon et
al.~\cite{Nixon2019} identified pathologies in the standard ECE
metric and proposed adaptive binning schemes (ACE) and a Generalized
Calibration Error (GCE) framework that conditions on class, uses
$L_2$ norms, and evaluates all class probabilities. Roelofs \emph{et
al.}~\cite{Roelofs2024} further showed that trivially
recalibrated models can appear state-of-the-art unless proper scoring
rules such as negative log-likelihood are jointly evaluated alongside
ECE.
% no logical link between these two paragraph
The earliest work addressing map matching reliability is Quddus
et al.~\cite{Quddus2006}, who proposed an empirical integrity
metric (0--100) for map-matched positions based on GPS quality
indicators, heading agreement, and map scale, achieving 98.2\% valid
integrity warnings using a fuzzy-logic algorithm with integrated
GPS/DR. Kempinska et al.~\cite{Kempinska2017} developed
Probabilistic ST-Matching , which outputs confidence
scores alongside point-to-edge matches, demonstrating that spatial
topological matching can be augmented with HMM-inspired posterior
probabilities. Wang et al.~\cite{Wang2023_PMHT} proposed a
Probabilistic Multiple-Hypotheses Tracker that weights
candidate locations by geometric distance to the prior and introduces
map feature variability as a statistical measure of local map
information density for confidence scoring. Phillips et
al.~\cite{Phillips2016} introduced Bayesian hypothesis testing for
verifying robotic geometry from point-cloud data, demonstrating that
reliable verification can be achieved by aggregating many independent
but individually weak measurements—a principle that motivates the
per-epoch trustworthiness accumulation in the present work.
% closer look at map matching related work
Bai and Hsu~\cite{Bai2023} integrated GNSS, visual, IMU, and map
constraints through sliding-window factor graph optimization,
achieving lane-level positioning in urban Hong Kong, though without a
formal trustworthiness metric. Quddus \emph{et
al.}~\cite{Quddus2007} noted as early as 2007 that the development
of integrity monitoring methods for map matching is an important
research direction. Yet, to our knowledge, no prior work has
systematically evaluated the \emph{probabilistic calibration} of HMM
map matching outputs through reliability diagrams and decomposition of
ECE by model component.

This work brings together calibration theory from machine
learning with integrity concepts from GNSS and the full HMM machinery
of map matching. We output a calibrated trustworthiness score (the
softmax-normalized smoothing posterior probabilistic) for every matched
epoch, evaluate its calibration through reliability diagrams % what is reliability diagrams
and ECE,
and decompose the calibration improvement via ablation studies to
quantify the per-component contribution of anisotropic 
emission model, posterior probabilistic with softmax, and integrity constraints. To our
knowledge, this is the first work to apply rigorous probabilistic
calibration analysis to map matching and to demonstrate that a
GNSS-uncertainty-consistent emission model reduces ECE by an order of
magnitude relative to the conventional isotropic approach.

\section{HMM-Based Map Matching: Classical Framework}
\label{sec:HMM_classic}
Hidden Markov Model (HMM)–based map matching is the most 
widely adopted approaches for inferring a vehicle's road position from noisy
GNSS observations, for its principled probabilistic
formulation and ability to incorporate topological constraints using the
road network graph. This section provides a concise introduction to the 
classical HMM map-matching pipeline, which forms the foundation of our
proposed covariance-aware framework.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Let $\{z_i\}_{i=1}^n$ denote the sequence of GNSS observations, and let
$\mathcal{C}_i = \{x_{i,1}, x_{i,2}, \ldots\}$ denote the candidate road
positions associated with observation $z_i$, constructed by projecting $z_i$
onto nearby road segments. The objective of map matching is to determine the
most probable sequence of road states
\[
X = (x_{1,j_1}, x_{2,j_2}, \ldots, x_{n,j_n}), \qquad x_{i,j} \in \mathcal{C}_i,
\]
that best explains the GNSS observations. According to Bayes’ rule, the optimal
solution is the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate:
\begin{equation}
    X^\star = \arg\max_X \, P(X \mid Z).
    \label{eq:HMM_MAP}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figs/example.png}
    \caption{Example of map matching problem}
    \label{fig:example}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Posterior Factorization and HMM Components}
Under the Markov assumption, the posterior in \eqref{eq:HMM_MAP} factorizes as
\begin{equation}
    P(X \mid Z) \propto
    \underbrace{\prod_{i=1}^n P(z_i \mid x_i)}_{\text{Emission}}
    \cdot
    \underbrace{\prod_{i=2}^n P(x_i \mid x_{i-1})}_{\text{Transition}}
    \cdot
    P(x_1),
    \label{eq:posterior_factorized}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $P(z_i \mid x_i)$ is the \emph{emission probability} describing how
          likely a GNSS fix is produced from candidate state $x_i$.
    \item $P(x_i \mid x_{i-1})$ is the \emph{transition probability} describing
          the feasibility of moving between researchen road states.
    \item $P(x_1)$ is the prior distribution for the initial state.
\end{itemize}

The optimal path is computed efficiently via the Viterbi algorithm. Thus,
traditional HMM map matching consists of two foundational components:
\textbf{emission modeling} and
\textbf{transition modeling}.

\subsection{Classical Hidden Markov Model based Map Matching}
\subsubsection{Classical Emission Probability}
In most existing HMM-based map-matching systems (e.g., FMM and many
OpenStreetMap toolchains), the emission probability is modeled as a
fixed, isotropic Gaussian:
\begin{equation}
    P(z_i \mid x_i)
    =
    \frac{1}{2\pi \sigma^2}
    \exp\!\left(
        -\frac{\|z_i - x_i\|^2}{2\sigma^2}
    \right),
    \label{eq:classic_emission}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is an empirically tuned GNSS error standard deviation that
is kept constant over time and space. This choice is attractive because
it is simple, computationally efficient, and easy to calibrate.
However, it assumes that GNSS errors are
(i) stationary in time,
(ii) isotropic in the horizontal plane, and
(iii) independent of the surrounding environment.
As a consequence, it does not exploit the rich uncertainty information
(such as covariance matrices or protection levels) that modern GNSS
receivers can provide.

\subsubsection{Classical Transition Probability}
In the classical formulation, transitions between successive candidate
states are typically modeled through a distance-consistency prior:
\begin{equation}
    P(x_i \mid x_{i-1})
    \propto
    \exp\!\left(
        -\frac{\left|
        d_{\text{road}}(x_{i-1}, x_i)
        -
        d_{\text{gnss}}(z_{i-1}, z_i)
        \right|}{\beta}
    \right),
    \label{eq:classic_transition}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\text{road}}$ is the shortest-path distance on the road graph,
$d_{\text{gnss}}$ is the GNSS-derived displacement between observations
$z_{i-1}$ and $z_i$, and $\beta$ is a scale parameter controlling how rapidly
the transition probability decays with increasing discrepancy between
network and GNSS distances. This model
favors road paths whose length is consistent with the observed GNSS
motion, while rejecting topologically infeasible transitions (e.g.,
driving against one-way restrictions or crossing disconnected links).

\subsubsection{Dynamic Programming via the Viterbi Algorithm}
Given the factorization in~\eqref{eq:posterior_factorized}, the optimal
state sequence is obtained using the Viterbi algorithm. By propagating
accumulated log-likelihoods forward in time and performing a single
backtracking pass, the algorithm reduces the combinatorial search
from $O(|\mathcal{C}|^n)$ to $O(n \cdot |\mathcal{C}|^2)$, which makes
real-time operation possible on large-scale road networks.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{figs/traditionHMM.png}
    \caption{Traditional HMM-based map-matching framework.}
    \label{fig:traditionHMM}
\end{figure}

The above emission and transition models, together with the Viterbi
recursion, constitute the classical HMM-based map-matching framework.
In the following subsection, limitations are discussed with respect
to GNSS stochastic modeling and integrity requirements, which motivates
the GNSS-consistent formulation developed in Section~\ref{sec:proposed}.


Although classical HMM-based map matching has proven effective in many
applications, its standard formulation exhibits several fundamental
limitations when viewed from the perspective of GNSS stochastic modeling
and integrity requirements. These limitations motivate the development of
the covariance-consistent, integrity-aware framework proposed in the next
section.

\paragraph{Fixed or heuristic candidate search radius.}
Most existing systems use either a constant buffer radius (e.g., 50--300\,m)
or heuristic iterative expansion to retrieve candidate road segments.
Such practices are disconnected from the actual GNSS positioning uncertainty:
the search radius does not enlarge under degraded satellite geometry or reduce
under high-precision conditions. As a result, the candidate set may
under-cover the true road segment in urban canyons or over-cover the network
in open-sky conditions, leading to unnecessary ambiguity and computational
overhead.

\paragraph{Euclidean projection assumes isotropic uncertainty.}
Candidate points are typically generated by orthogonally projecting the GNSS
fix onto nearby road polylines. This implicitly assumes that the GNSS error
distribution is spherical and direction-independent. In reality, GNSS error
ellipses are highly anisotropic and rotate with satellite geometry, multipath,
and blockage. Euclidean projection therefore selects the geometrically closest
point rather than the statistically most plausible one, causing systematic
degradation in challenging environments.

\paragraph{Isotropic emission probability ignores covariance and protection levels.}
Classical emission models adopt a fixed Gaussian likelihood
$\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I)$, which disregards both
(i) the time-varying covariance matrix provided by modern receivers and  
(ii) the integrity information encoded in protection levels (PLs).
This prevents the HMM from adapting to changes in GNSS quality and fails to
capture the true stochastic structure of the measurement process.

\paragraph{Absence of a principled trustworthiness or integrity indicator.}
The classical HMM returns only the most probable path but does not quantify
how reliable this solution is. In dense urban road networks, multiple
topologically valid paths may be consistent with the GNSS observations, and
the classical framework provides no mechanism to assess ambiguity or detect
potential mismatch events. This limitation is critical for safety-related
applications---such as road pricing, autonomous driving, or integrity
monitoring---where reliability must be quantified, not assumed.

\section{GNSS-Consistent HMM and Trustworthiness Evaluation}
\label{sec:proposed}
This section presents an enhanced Hidden Markov Model (HMM)–based map-matching formulation called \textbf{Covariance-aware Map Matching using HMM } that incorporates GNSS positioning uncertainty at three levels: candidate search region definition, candidate point generation, and emission probability modeling. A final subsection introduces a trustworthiness statistic derived from the joint likelihood of the optimal HMM path, enabling consistency evaluation between GNSS observations and the road network. For brevity, the proposed method is referred to as \textbf{CMM} in the remainder of this paper.

\subsection{GNSS Stochastic Error model and Protection Level}

\subsubsection{GNSS positioning and state covariance}
% WLS formulation, state covariance Σ_z, horizontal covariance Σ_i

The most common positioning method is single-point positioning (SPP). The GNSS position solution and its associated
uncertainty are obtained through a weighted least-squares (WLS) estimation
based on pseudorange observations. 
%========================================================
% WLS covariance derivation for GNSS pseudorange positioning
%========================================================

\subsubsection{Weighted least-squares solution and covariance of GNSS positioning}

\paragraph{Observation model.}
Assume $m$ satellites are tracked at an epoch. The pseudorange observation vector is
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\rho}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\rho_1 & \rho_2 & \cdots & \rho_m
\end{bmatrix}^{\mathrm{T}},
\end{equation}
and the unknown state is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
x & y & z & \delta t
\end{bmatrix}^{\mathrm{T}},
\end{equation}
where $(x,y,z)$ is the receiver position in ECEF and $\delta t$ denotes the receiver clock bias in meters.

For satellite $i$, the linearized pseudo range model can be written as
\begin{equation}
\rho_i
=
r_i(\mathbf{x}) + \delta t + \varepsilon_i,
\end{equation}
where $r_i(\mathbf{x})$ is the geometric range and $\varepsilon_i$ is the measurement noise.

Stacking all measurements yields
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\rho}
=
\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon},
\qquad
\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}]=\mathbf{0},
\qquad
\operatorname{cov}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})=\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}.
\label{eq:rho_model}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Linearization.}
Let $\mathbf{x}_0$ be the linearization point and define the increment $\Delta\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_0$.
Using first-order Taylor expansion,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x})
\approx
\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}_0) + \mathbf{H}\,\Delta\mathbf{x},
\end{equation}
where the Jacobian (geometry) matrix is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{H}
=
\left.\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x})}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\right|_{\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}_0}
\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times 4}.
\end{equation}

Define the pre-fit residual (innovation) vector
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}
=
\boldsymbol{\rho}-\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}_0).
\end{equation}
Then the linearized model becomes
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}
=
\mathbf{H}\,\Delta\mathbf{x} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}.
\label{eq:lin_model}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Weighted least-squares estimator.}
Given the weight matrix $\mathbf{W}=\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}^{-1}$, the WLS estimate is obtained by minimizing
\begin{equation}
J(\Delta\mathbf{x})
=
(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{H}\Delta\mathbf{x})^{\mathrm{T}}
\mathbf{W}
(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{H}\Delta\mathbf{x}).
\end{equation}
Setting $\partial J/\partial \Delta\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$ yields the normal equation
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H}\,\widehat{\Delta\mathbf{x}}
=
\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{y},
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\Delta\mathbf{x}}
=
(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H})^{-1}
\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{y}.
\label{eq:wls_dx}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Estimation error covariance.}
From \eqref{eq:lin_model} and \eqref{eq:wls_dx}, the estimation error is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\Delta\mathbf{x}}
=
\widehat{\Delta\mathbf{x}}-\Delta\mathbf{x}
=
(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H})^{-1}
\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}.
\end{equation}
Thus, using $\operatorname{cov}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})=\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}$ and $\mathbf{W}=\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}^{-1}$,

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned} 
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{x}
&=
\operatorname{cov}(\widetilde{\Delta\mathbf{x}})\\
&=
(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H})^{-1}
\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\,
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}\,
\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H}
(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H})^{-1}\\
&=
(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}^{-1}\mathbf{H})^{-1}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:wls_cov_full}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{4D and 3D covariance forms.}
If the estimated state is $\mathbf{x}=[x,y,z,\delta t]^{\mathrm{T}}$, then
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{x}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{pos}} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{pos},t}\\
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{t,\text{pos}} & \sigma_t^2
\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
where the $3\times 3$ position covariance is the upper-left block:
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{pos}}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_x^2 & \sigma_{xy} & \sigma_{xz}\\
\sigma_{xy} & \sigma_y^2 & \sigma_{yz}\\
\sigma_{xz} & \sigma_{yz} & \sigma_z^2
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:pos_cov_block}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figs/covariance.png}
    \caption{Visual representation of the GNSS positioning covariance calculation process. The final position uncertainty (Right) is a function of the satellite geometry matrix $\mathbf{H}$ (Left) and the pseudorange measurement noise $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\rho}$ (Middle). This process results in the anisotropic error ellipse $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ used in our probability emission model.}
    \label{fig:covariance}
\end{figure}

In practical GNSS receivers, this covariance matrix is often made available
through standard output messages such as the NMEA \texttt{GST} sentence, which
provides axis-aligned or rotated confidence ellipse parameters that can be
converted into $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pos}$. This covariance encapsulates the
combined effects of satellite geometry, atmospheric errors, multipath, and
measurement noise, and therefore serves as the statistical basis for the
covariance-aware emission probability model introduced in the following
subsection.

\subsubsection{Integrity monitoring and horizontal protection level}
% RAIM/ARAIM description, PL / HPL_i, final r_i = HPL_i
Protection Level (PL) is a widely adopted integrity metric that provides a conservative upper bound on the horizontal position error for a specified integrity risk level (e.g., $10^{-5}$ or $10^{-7}$). The protection level (PL) denotes the threshold below which the true horizontal position error lies with integrity risk not exceeding $10^{-5}$, that is,
\begin{equation}
\Pr\!\left( |e_{\mathrm{hor}}| > \mathrm{PL} \right) \le 10^{-5}.
\label{eq:protection level}
\end{equation}

In integrity monitoring, the PL is an alternative representation of the integrity risk bound. Rather than directly computing the probability of hazardous misleading information (HMI), the PL expresses the maximum position-domain error that would remain safe under a specified integrity requirement. This makes PL a more practical and receiver-agnostic quantity, especially when the alert limits (ALs) of downstream applications are unknown to the GNSS receiver.

The PL corresponding to the integrity risk expression can be computed iteratively using the nonlinear equation

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{I_{REQ}}
&<
2\, Q\!\left( \frac{\mathrm{PL} - b_0}{\sigma_0} \right)
+
\sum_{i=1}^{h}
Q\!\left(
\frac{\mathrm{PL} - T\Delta_i - b_i}{\sigma_i}
\right) P_{H_i}  \\
&\quad
+\, P_{NM},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:pl_iterative}
\end{equation}

where $Q(\cdot)$ is the standard Gaussian tail probability, $(b_0, \sigma_0)$ denote the nominal bias and standard deviation, $(b_i, \sigma_i)$ and $P_{H_i}$ are the corresponding parameters under fault mode $i$, $T\Delta_i$ represents the solution-separation term for the $i$-th fault hypothesis, and $P_{NM}$ is the probability of non-monitored fault modes. 

The resulting PL encapsulates the same integrity information as the underlying risk model but in a form readily usable by downstream safety-critical applications. In this work, the Horizontal Protection Level (HPL) obtained from \eqref{eq:pl_iterative} is used as the radius of the candidate search region in covariance-aware map matching.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figs/PL.png}
    \caption{The illustration of protection level}
    \label{fig:PL}
\end{figure}

Two major algorithms exist for computing HPL:

\paragraph{Residual-based HPL (RAIM)}
In classical Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring (RAIM), GNSS measurements are checked for consistency using residual tests. Let $\mathbf{H}$ denote the geometry matrix and $\mathbf{W}$ the weighting matrix. The position-domain covariance is obtained as
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{pos}}
=
\left( \mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{W} \mathbf{H} \right)^{-1}.
\end{equation}
The horizontal standard deviation is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{H}}
=
\sqrt{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2},
\end{equation}
and the protection level is computed as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{HPL}_{\mathrm{RAIM}}
=
K_{\mathrm{RAIM}} \, \sigma_{\mathrm{H}},
\end{equation}

where $K_{\mathrm{RAIM}}$ depends on the false-alarm and missed-detection probabilities. This method is computationally simple but assumes a single-fault model with Gaussian errors.

\paragraph{MHSS-based HPL (ARAIM).}

Advanced RAIM (ARAIM) adopts the Multiple Hypothesis Solution Separation (MHSS) framework, which explicitly accounts for multiple satellite fault hypotheses. For each fault mode $f \in \mathcal{F}$, a solution-separation vector is computed. The horizontal protection level is then

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{HPL}_{\mathrm{MHSS}}
=
\max_{f \in \mathcal{F}}
\left(
| S_f | 
+
K_f \, \sigma_{\mathrm{H},f}
\right),
\end{equation}
where $S_f$ is the solution-separation bias, $\sigma_{\mathrm{H},f}$ is the protected standard deviation under mode $f$, and $K_f$ is the integrity coefficient for that mode. This method provides statistically rigorous integrity guarantees and incorporates fault geometry explicitly.


\subsection{GNSS-Consistent HMM Map Matching}
\label{subsec:gnss_consistent_hmm}

This section reformulates the HMM map-matching framework so that all core
elements---states, observations, candidate generation, and emission
likelihoods---are explicitly consistent with the stochastic GNSS error model.
Unlike the classical formulation, which assumes fixed isotropic uncertainty,
the proposed framework incorporates the full covariance information
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ (or equivalently PL-based bounds) provided by modern GNSS
receivers. This establishes a unified probabilistic treatment of uncertainty
throughout the entire HMM pipeline.

\subsubsection{States and Observations with Covariance}
\label{subsubsec:states_observations}

Let
\[
z_i \in \mathbb{R}^2
\]
denote the horizontal GNSS position estimate at epoch $i$, and let
\[
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{2\times 2}
\]
be the corresponding GNSS positioning covariance matrix obtained from the
weighted least-squares (WLS) solution or receiver-reported confidence ellipse
(e.g., NMEA \texttt{GST}). The pair $(z_i, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i)$ therefore
represents a stochastic observation whose uncertainty is explicitly modeled.

Given the uncertainty-aware candidate search region (e.g., the
HPL-based disk $\Omega_i$), the set of feasible road segments for epoch $i$
is
\[
\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{seg},i}
=
\{\, s \in \mathcal{G} : s \cap \Omega_i \neq \varnothing \,\}.
\]

Each state in the HMM corresponds to a specific candidate point obtained by
projecting the GNSS observation onto one of these segments using the
Mahalanobis projection operator:
\[
x_{i,j}
\in
\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i}
=
\Big\{
\arg\min_{x \in s_j}
f(z_i - x)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1} (z_i - x)
\;\Big|\;
s_j \in \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{seg},i}
\Big\}.
\]

Thus, for each epoch $i$, the HMM state set is the uncertainty-consistent
candidate collection
\[
\mathcal{C}_{pnt,i} = \{ x_{i,1}, x_{i,2}, \ldots \},
\]
and the emission probability model and transition model defined in later
subsections operate entirely on this covariance-aware state representation.

By embedding GNSS covariance directly into the observation model and candidate
generation process, the resulting HMM formulation provides a statistically
accurate description of the measurement process and forms the basis for both
improved map-matching accuracy and integrity-aware trustworthiness evaluation.



\subsubsection{HPL-based candidate segment search}
% Your candidate road segment search using Ω_i and HPL_i
Conventional HMM-based map matching methods typically retrieve candidate road segments by applying a fixed-radius buffer around each GNSS observation. Such a static search range does not account for the time-varying nature of GNSS measurement quality, which is strongly influenced by satellite geometry, multipath, and environmental occlusions. In the formulation of Paul \cite{HMM}, for example, a constant search radius of 300 m is employed regardless of the underlying positioning uncertainty. Some subsequent studies introduce heuristic, iteratively expanded search radii to control computational complexity by maintaining the number of retrieved candidate segments within a predefined range. Under sparse road conditions, the search radius is enlarged (e.g., doubled) to ensure a sufficient number of candidate states, whereas in dense urban networks the radius is reduced to avoid an excessive candidate set. Although such adaptive heuristics provide partial mitigation, they remain detached from the physical stochastic properties of GNSS measurements and therefore lack principled statistical grounding.

To address this, we introduce an \textbf{adaptive, integrity-driven search region} based on the horizontal protection level (HPL). Conventional fixed-radius candidate search fails to reflect real-time variations in GNSS positioning uncertainty. In this study, the search region is determined using the \textbf{Horizontal Protection Level (HPL)}. Given the horizontal protection level (HPL) estimated by either RAIM or ARAIM,
the candidate search radius at epoch $i$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
r_i = \mathrm{HPL}_i .
\end{equation}
By definition, the HPL guarantees that the true horizontal position error does
not exceed $r_i$ with the specified integrity risk. Therefore, all road segments
that intersect the corresponding HPL-based search region are considered valid
candidates for map matching at epoch $i$.

Let $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^2$ denote the GNSS position estimate at epoch $i$, and
define the HPL-induced search region as the closed disk
\begin{equation}
\Omega_i
=
\left\{
x \in \mathbb{R}^2 \;\middle|\; \| x - z_i \|_2 \le r_i
\right\},
\end{equation}
where $\| \cdot \|_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm in the horizontal plane.

Let $\mathcal{G}$ denote the road network, represented as a set of road segments
embedded in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The candidate road segment set for epoch $i$ is then
defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{\text{seg},i}
=
\left\{
s \in \mathcal{G}
\;\middle|\;
s \cap \Omega_i \neq \varnothing
\right\},
\label{eq:candidate_set}
\end{equation}
i.e., all road segments that geometrically intersect the HPL-based search region.

This HPL-driven candidate selection strategy ensures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item expanded search regions under degraded GNSS conditions,
    \item compact and efficient search under open-sky environments,
    \item integrity-consistent and parameter-free candidate generation
          without ad hoc tuning.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Mahalanobis-based candidate point projection}
% Your Mahalanobis projection section and figures
Traditional HMM-based map-matching algorithms compute candidate points by performing a Euclidean orthogonal projection of each GNSS observation onto nearby road polylines. This procedure implicitly assumes an \emph{isotropic} GNSS error model, meaning that the uncertainty is the same in all directions. However, real GNSS positioning errors are highly \emph{anisotropic}, producing elongated covariance ellipses whose principal axes vary with satellite geometry and multipath.

To account for this anisotropy, we adopt the \textbf{Mahalanobis projection} operator, which projects GNSS observations onto road segments in a manner consistent with the covariance structure. The Mahalanobis projection admits an equivalent geometric interpretation that further clarifies its advantage over Euclidean projection. Let $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i = \mathbf{L}_i \mathbf{L}_i^{\mathrm{T}}$ be the Cholesky factorization of the GNSS covariance matrix. By applying the linear whitening transformation

\begin{equation}
\tilde{z}_i = \mathbf{L}_i^{-1} z_i, 
\quad
\tilde{x} = \mathbf{L}_i^{-1} x
\end{equation}

the anisotropic covariance ellipse in the original coordinate frame is mapped to an isotropic unit circle in the whitened space. Under this transformation, the Mahalanobis distance satisfies

\begin{equation}
    \left(z_i - x\right)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1} \left(z_i - x\right)
=
\|\tilde{z}_i - \tilde{x}\|_2^{\,2}
\end{equation}

which means that Mahalanobis projection in the original coordinates is exactly equivalent to Euclidean orthogonal projection in the whitened coordinates. Thus, the proposed approach can be interpreted as performing projection after shaping the coordinate frame so that the GNSS uncertainty becomes spherical. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figs/maha_vs_eu.png}
    \caption{Illustration of Euclidean projection versus Mahalanobis projection under an anisotropic GNSS covariance ellipse. }
    \label{fig:maha_vs_eu}
\end{figure}

This perspective highlights that the Mahalanobis projection is the statistically appropriate projection operator when GNSS errors are anisotropic, whereas Euclidean projection implicitly assumes a spherical uncertainty model that is generally violated in practical GNSS environments. Fig.~\ref{fig:measprob} provides a more detailed illustration of the emission probability under the anisotropic covariance model.

The candidates are generated as following steps. For the $i\ th$ GNSS observation $z_i$ and corresponding candidate road segment $s_j$ in the segment candidates set , the projected point is defined as
\begin{equation}
x_{i,j}
=
\arg\min_{x \in s_j}
\left( z_i - x \right)^{\mathrm{T}}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}
\left( z_i - x \right),
\label{eq:ma_project}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ is the $2\times 2$ horizontal covariance matrix reported by the GNSS receiver.

The resulting set of candidate points is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{pnt,i}
=
\left\{
x_{i,j}
\;\middle|\;
j:r_j \in \mathcal{C}_{seg,i}
\right\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}_{seg,i}$ denotes the set of candidate segments obtained from the HPL-based search region. This covariance-consistent projection provides several advantages:
\begin{itemize}
    \item it aligns the projection direction with the principal axes of the GNSS error ellipse;
    \item it avoids selecting candidates that lie along high-uncertainty axes, even if they are geometrically closer;
    \item it naturally adapts to time-varying and environment-dependent GNSS uncertainty statistics.
\end{itemize}

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figs/measprob.png}
    \caption{Illustration of Euclidean projection versus Mahalanobis projection under an anisotropic GNSS covariance ellipse. 
    The red point denotes the GNSS observation $z_i$; 
    the yellow point is the Euclidean (orthogonal) projection onto the road segment; 
    the green point is the Mahalanobis projection that minimizes the covariance-weighted distance. 
    Because the GNSS error ellipse is elongated, the Mahalanobis projection correctly selects the candidate aligned with the principal axis of uncertainty, whereas the Euclidean projection incorrectly favors the geometrically closest point.}

\subsubsection{Covariance-based Gaussian emission probability}
% Your emission probability with Σ_i and discussion (shorter; refer back to 3.1 for Σ_i)
Given an observation $z_i$ and a candidate state $x_{i,j}$, the emission
probability is modeled using a bivariate Gaussian distribution governed by the
GNSS covariance matrix:
\begin{equation}
p(z_i \mid x_{i,j})
=
\frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i|}}
\exp\!\left[
-\frac{1}{2}
\mathbf{d}_{i,j}^{\mathrm{T}}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}
\mathbf{d}_{i,j}
\right],
\label{eq:cov_emission}
\end{equation}
where the positioning residual is
\[
\mathbf{d}_{i,j} = z_i - x_{i,j}.
\]

Compared with the classical fixed-variance isotropic likelihood
$\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2I)$, the covariance-based model incorporates the
full GNSS error geometry and therefore

\begin{itemize}
    \item adapts automatically to real-time GNSS quality,
    \item tightens the likelihood when satellite geometry is good, 
    \item avoids overconfidence in degraded environments,
    \item remains statistically consistent with the underlying GNSS stochastic model.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Probabilistic Normalization of the HMM}
\label{subsec:prob_normalization}

For the trustworthiness scores to be calibrated probabilities, all HMM
parameters must be properly normalized.

\paragraph{Emission probability with background state.}
The raw Gaussian density from Eq.~\eqref{eq:cov_emission} is scaled to
account for the possibility that the vehicle is not on any mapped road.
A background pseudo-candidate $b$ with fixed emission probability
$p_{\text{bg}}$ (default 0.1) represents the ``off-road'' hypothesis.
For each real candidate $j$, the effective emission is
\begin{equation}
p'(z_i \mid x_{i,j}) = (1 - p_{\text{bg}}) \cdot p(z_i \mid x_{i,j}),
\qquad
p'(z_i \mid b) = p_{\text{bg}}.
\label{eq:bg_emission}
\end{equation}
This prevents overconfidence when all road candidates have low likelihood,
since the background state absorbs probability mass in the softmax
denominator.

\paragraph{Transition probability row normalization.}
The raw transition weight between candidates $a$ and $b$ follows the
standard distance-consistency model:
\[
w_{a \to b} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{|d_{\text{road}}(a,b) - d_{\text{gnss}}(z_{t-1},z_t)|}{\beta}\right).
\]
To ensure $\sum_j t_{a \to j} = 1$ for each source candidate $a$, the
normalized transition is
\begin{equation}
t_{a \to b} = \frac{w_{a \to b}}{\sum_{j} w_{a \to j}}.
\label{eq:tp_normalized}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Uniform initial prior.}
Without prior knowledge, the initial state distribution is uniform over
the $K$ real candidates:
\begin{equation}
\pi(i) = \frac{1}{K}, \qquad
\log \alpha_1(i) = \log\frac{1}{K} + \log p(z_1 \mid s_1^{(i)}).
\label{eq:uniform_prior}
\end{equation}
These three normalization steps ensure that the forward variable
$\alpha_t$ and the resulting trustworthiness scores are valid probabilities
suitable for calibration analysis.

\subsection{Forward Recursions and Trustworthiness Computation}

Given the observation sequence $Z=\{z_1,\ldots,z_N\}$ and the HMM
defined above, let $\alpha_t(i) = P(z_{1:t}, s_t^{(i)})$ denote the
forward probability, computed via the standard HMM recursion in
log-space:
\begin{equation}
\log \alpha_t(b)
= \log \sum_{a} \exp\!\big(\log \alpha_{t-1}(a) + \log t_{a \to b}\big)
+ \log p(z_t \mid s_t^{(b)}),
\label{eq:log_forward}
\end{equation}
where the initial condition is
$\log \alpha_1(i) = \log\pi(i) + \log p(z_1 \mid s_1^{(i)})$ with the
uniform prior $\pi(i) = 1/K$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:uniform_prior}).

Similarly, the Viterbi (maximum) probability
$\delta_t(i) = \max_{s_{1:t-1}} P(z_{1:t}, s_{1:t-1}, s_t^{(i)})$
is:
\begin{equation}
\log \delta_t(b)
= \max_{a}\!\big(\log \delta_{t-1}(a) + \log t_{a \to b}\big)
+ \log p(z_t \mid s_t^{(b)}).
\label{eq:log_viterbi}
\end{equation}
Because the maximum over paths cannot exceed the sum,
$\delta_t(i) \leq \alpha_t(i)$ holds for all $t$ and $i$. The gap
$\alpha_t(i) - \delta_t(i)$ encodes the cumulative ambiguity from
competing paths converging to the same candidate.

All probability parameters---emission $p(z_t \mid s_t^{(b)})$ (with
background state, Eq.~\eqref{eq:bg_emission}), transition
$t_{a \to b}$ (row-normalized, Eq.~\eqref{eq:tp_normalized}), and
prior $\pi$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:uniform_prior})---are properly normalized
as described in Section~\ref{subsec:prob_normalization}.

\subsubsection{Marginal Filtering Posterior (Per-Candidate)}

The marginal posterior probability of candidate $i$ at epoch $t$
given all past observations is:
\begin{equation}
P(s_t^{(i)} \mid z_{1:t})
= \frac{\alpha_t(i)}{\sum_{j} \alpha_t(j)},
\label{eq:filtering_posterior}
\end{equation}
where the denominator sums over all $K$ real candidates plus the
background pseudo-candidate.  This quantity, reported as
\texttt{n\_best\_trustworthiness}, represents the probability that
the vehicle occupies edge $i$ at epoch $t$ considering all possible
paths through the trellis.

\subsubsection{Joint Path Posterior (Trustworthiness)}

The joint posterior probability of the Viterbi-optimal partial path
$H^*_{1:t} = (s_1^*, \dots, s_t^*)$ is:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P(H^*_{1:t} \mid z_{1:t})
&= \frac{\delta_t(i^*)}{\sum_j \alpha_t(j)}
= \exp\!\big(\log \delta_t(i^*) - \log\!\!\sum_j \exp(\log \alpha_t(j))\big),
\label{eq:joint_posterior}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $i^* = \arg\max_j \delta_t(j)$ is the Viterbi-optimal candidate
at epoch $t$.  This is the \emph{trustworthiness} score
$\text{tw}_t \in [0,1]$.

Because $\delta_t \leq \alpha_t$, the joint path posterior is always
$\leq$ the marginal posterior.  When many competing paths converge to
the same Viterbi winner---as occurs at dense intersections or on
parallel roads---$\alpha_t$ grows large while $\delta_t$ stays
bounded, causing trustworthiness to drop sharply even when the
marginal posterior remains near 1.0.  This sensitivity to cumulative
path ambiguity makes trustworthiness a more conservative and
informative mismatch detector than the per-candidate marginal.

\subsubsection{Fixed-Lag Smoothing (Optional)}
\label{subsec:lag_smoothing}

To incorporate future evidence and sharpen the posterior, the forward
computation can be extended with a \emph{fixed-lag smoothing}
mechanism.  When a smoothing lag $L > 0$ is configured, a buffer of
$L$ future layers is maintained.  For each candidate $i$ at the oldest
buffered layer $t$, a Viterbi-style forward pass through the $L$-step
look-ahead window accumulates the best achievable future path score:
\begin{equation}
\beta_{t+L}(i)
= \max_{\{s_{t+1},\dots,s_{t+L}\}}
\sum_{\tau=t+1}^{t+L}
\Big[ \log t_{s_{\tau-1} \to s_\tau}
     + \log p(z_\tau \mid s_\tau) \Big],
\label{eq:viterbi_future}
\end{equation}
and the smoothed score becomes
$\text{score}_t(i) = \log \alpha_t(i) + \beta_{t+L}(i)$,
with softmax normalization as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:filtering_posterior}.
On real data with RAIM-derived PLs, the optimal lag is $L=0$
(see Section~\ref{sec:simulation}), indicating that the protection
level already encodes sufficient geometric uncertainty and that the
anisotropic emission model provides adequate discriminative power
without temporal smoothing.

\subsubsection{Information-Theoretic Metrics}

To complement trustworthiness, we compute the posterior entropy
$H_t = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i$, where
$p_i = P(s_t^{(i)} \mid z_{1:t})$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:filtering_posterior}.
The information gain (delta entropy) relative to the prior distribution is
\begin{equation}
\Delta H_t = H_{\text{prior}}^{t} - H_t,
\label{eq:delta_entropy}
\end{equation}
where $H_{\text{prior}}^{t} = \log_2(K_t)$ for the first epoch
(uniform prior), and for subsequent epochs $H_{\text{prior}}^{t}$ is the
entropy of the predictive distribution
$P(s_t^{(b)} \mid z_{1:t-1})
 \propto \sum_a P(s_{t-1}^{(a)} \mid z_{1:t-1}) \, t_{a \to b}$.
A large $\Delta H_t$ indicates that the emission probabilities
strongly resolve the uncertainty propagated from the previous layer;
$\Delta H_t \approx 0$ suggests the GNSS observation provides little
additional evidence, flagging potentially ambiguous epochs.


\subsection{Summary of GNSS-Consistent HMM Formulation and Trustworthiness}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{CMM Map Matching with Probabilistic Trustworthiness}
\label{alg:cmm_hmm}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Road network $\mathcal{G}$, trajectory $Z=\{z_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, covariance $\{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, HPL $\{r_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, background prob. $p_{\text{bg}}$, lag $L$, UBODT $\mathcal{U}$
\State \textbf{Output:} Optimal path $X^\star$, trustworthiness $\{\text{tw}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, n-best $\{\text{nb}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, entropy $\{H_i, \Delta H_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$
\State Initialize lag buffer $\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \varnothing$, candidate sets $\mathcal{C}_{1 \dots N} \leftarrow \varnothing$
\For{$i = 1, 2, \dots, N$}
    \State Define search region $\Omega_i \leftarrow \{ x \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \|x - z_i\|_2 \le r_i \}$
    \State $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{seg},i} \leftarrow \{ s \in \mathcal{G} : s \cap \Omega_i \neq \varnothing \}$
    \If{$\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{seg},i} = \varnothing$}
        \State Mark epoch as gap; \textbf{continue}
    \EndIf
    \For{\textbf{each} $s_j \in \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{seg},i}$}
        \State $x_{i,j} \leftarrow \arg\min_{x \in s_j} (z_i - x)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1} (z_i - x)$ \label{alg:ma_proj}
        \State $\log p(z_i \mid x_{i,j}) \leftarrow -\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i|) - \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{d}_{i,j}^{\mathrm{T}}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}\mathbf{d}_{i,j}$ \label{alg:emission}
        \State $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i} \leftarrow \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i} \cup \{x_{i,j}\}$
    \EndFor
    \State Append background pseudo-candidate $b$ with $\log p(z_i \mid b) \leftarrow \log p_{\text{bg}}$ to $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i}$
    \State Compute normalized transition weights $\log t_{a \to b}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:tp_normalized}) between $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i-1}$ and $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i}$
    \State $\log \alpha_i(b) \leftarrow \log\!\sum_{a}\!\exp(\log\alpha_{i-1}(a)+\log t_{a\to b}) + \log p(z_i\mid s_i^{(b)})$ \label{alg:forward}
    \State $\log \delta_i(b) \leftarrow \max_{a}[ \log\delta_{i-1}(a) + \log t_{a\to b}] + \log p(z_i\mid s_i^{(b)})$ \label{alg:viterbi}
    \If{$i = 1$}
        \State $\log \alpha_1(i) \leftarrow \log(1/K) + \log p(z_1\mid s_1^{(i)})$; $\log \delta_1(i) \leftarrow \log(1/K) + \log p(z_1\mid s_1^{(i)})$ \label{alg:init}
    \EndIf
    \If{$L > 0$}
        \State Push $\text{layer}_i = \{\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{pnt},i}, \log\alpha_i, \log\delta_i, \log t\}$ to buffer $\mathcal{B}$
        \If{$|\mathcal{B}| > L$}
            \State Let $\ell$ be oldest layer; compute $\beta_{\ell+L}(j)$ via Viterbi forward \label{alg:viterbi_fwd}
            \State $\text{score}_\ell(j) \leftarrow \log\alpha_\ell(j) + \beta_{\ell+L}(j)$
            \State $p_j \leftarrow \exp(\text{score}_\ell(j)) / \sum_k \exp(\text{score}_\ell(k))$
            \State $\text{tw}_\ell \leftarrow \max_j p_j$; \quad $H_\ell \leftarrow -\sum_j p_j\log_2 p_j$
            \State Pop oldest layer from $\mathcal{B}$
        \EndIf
    \Else
        \State $\text{tw}_i \leftarrow \exp(\log\delta_i(i^*)) / \sum_j \exp(\log\alpha_i(j))$ \quad (joint path posterior) \label{alg:tw}
        \State $\text{nb}_i \leftarrow \exp(\log\alpha_i(j)) / \sum_k \exp(\log\alpha_i(k))$ \quad (marginal posterior) \label{alg:nbest}
        \State $H_i \leftarrow -\sum_j p_j\log_2 p_j$; $\Delta H_i \leftarrow H_{\text{prior}} - H_i$
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\If{$L > 0$}
    \State \textbf{Flush buffer:} repeat smoothing for remaining layers with decreasing effective lag
\EndIf
\State Backtrack via stored backpointers to retrieve optimal path $X^\star$
\State \textbf{return} $X^\star$, $\{\text{tw}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, $\{\text{nb}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$, $\{H_i, \Delta H_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$
\end{algorithmic}
    \vspace{-10pt}
\end{algorithm}


By incorporating integrity-aware candidate search based on protection level
and covariance-aware emission probability modeling, an enhanced HMM-based
map-matching framework is obtained. Under this formulation, the most probable
road-constrained trajectory is determined by maximizing the joint likelihood
between the GNSS observations and the road-network-constrained state sequence.
From a Bayesian perspective, this procedure already yields a statistically
optimal map-matching solution in the maximum \emph{a posteriori} (MAP) sense.

However, for safety-critical applications such as navigation charging,
autonomous driving support, or integrity monitoring, providing only a
single best-matching result is insufficient. In addition to the estimated road
trajectory, the navigation system must also quantify how reliable this result
is. In practice, map-matching failures tend to occur in environments where
GNSS observations are degraded and multiple topologically plausible road
sequences are simultaneously consistent with the measurements. Therefore,
the \emph{trustworthiness} of a map-matching result should explicitly reflect
the degree of consistency between GNSS observations and the underlying road
network constraints.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{figs/trustworthiness.png}
    \caption{Trustworthiness in different road segments}
    \label{fig:trustworthiness}
\end{figure}

The joint probability inherent to the HMM formulation naturally encodes this
consistency and thus provides a principled basis for trustworthiness
evaluation. Nevertheless, raw likelihood values are sensitive to scale effects
introduced by covariance magnitude, candidate set size, and trajectory length.
To enable meaningful comparison across epochs and scenarios, additional
normalization and interpretation are required.

\section{Simulation Study}
\label{sec:simulation}

This section presents five experiments designed to systematically evaluate the proposed covariance-aware HMM framework. Experiment~1 validates the internal consistency of the covariance-based emission model through statistical goodness-of-fit tests. Experiment~2 assesses the RAIM-derived Protection Level (PL) coverage via Stanford plots and quantifies Fault Detection and Exclusion (FDE) performance under injected step faults and cross-road occlusion. Experiment~3 conducts a comprehensive parameter sensitivity sweep, comparing CMM against FMM across pseudorange noise levels $\sigma_{\rho}\in[1,30]$\,m and sample rates $f_s\in[0.1,1.0]$\,Hz using segment-level accuracy, Expected Calibration Error (ECE), and ROC/AUC. Experiment~4 analyzes the effect of a misspecified emission model, where the WLS solver assumes a fixed $\sigma_{\text{wls}}=20$\,m while the true pseudorange noise varies from 10 to 30\,m. Experiment~5 evaluates robustness under combined degradation: step fault injection and cross-road satellite occlusion at $\sigma=30$\,m.

All experiments share the Haikou, Hainan road network (\texttt{input/map/hainan/edges.shp}, EPSG-projected) and a precomputed UBODT table. Both CMM and FMM are configured with $k=16$ candidates per epoch. CMM uses a protection-level multiplier of 3, PHMI $=10^{-5}$, a fixed smoothing lag of $L=5$, a background probability $p_{\text{bg}}=0.1$, and a window length of 100 epochs; FMM uses a fixed search radius $r=0.03^\circ$ and isotropic GPS error $\sigma=0.001^\circ$, following the standard configuration in~\cite{FMM}. For the real-vehicle experiments (Section~\ref{sec:real-experiment}), CMM uses $L=0$ as the optimal configuration given the tight RAIM-derived protection levels.

\subsection{Simulation Environment and Data Generation}
\label{sec:sim_environment}

\subsubsection{Ground-Truth Trajectory Generation}

Ground-truth vehicle trajectories are generated by randomly sampling connected road segments from the Haikou road network. A vehicle is simulated moving along the selected path at a constant speed of 12\,m/s, with linear velocity changes (magnitude and direction) at turns and intersections. Each trajectory contains 1000 epochs sampled at 1\,Hz. For each pseudorange noise level $\sigma_{\rho}$, 20 independent trajectories are generated, yielding approximately 20,000 matched epochs per noise condition. The road segments traversed by each trajectory are recorded, providing per-epoch ground-truth edge IDs for segment-level accuracy evaluation.

\subsubsection{Synthetic GNSS Constellation and Pseudorange Observations}

For each trajectory, a synthetic satellite constellation of 8 satellites is generated with uniformly random azimuths in $[0^\circ,360^\circ)$ and elevations in $[45^\circ,80^\circ)$, simulating a typical open-sky mid-latitude geometry. Pseudorange observations are synthesized by computing the true geometric range from each satellite to the ground-truth vehicle position and adding zero-mean Gaussian noise with standard deviation $\sigma_{\rho}$, following the observation model of Eq.~\eqref{eq:rho_model}:

\begin{equation}
\rho_i = r_i(\mathbf{x}_{\text{true}}) + \delta t + \varepsilon_i,\quad
\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\rho}^2).
\label{eq:sim_pr}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Weighted Least-Squares Positioning with RAIM-FDE}

Vehicle position and its associated $2\times 2$ horizontal covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ are estimated via the weighted least-squares (WLS) solution of Eq.~\eqref{eq:wls_dx}--\eqref{eq:wls_cov_full}. The pseudorange weight matrix is $\mathbf{W} = \sigma_{\rho}^{-2}\mathbf{I}$.

A Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring (RAIM) module performs two functions. First, a global consistency test checks the sum of squared residuals against a $\chi^2$ threshold at false-alarm probability $P_{\text{FA}}=10^{-5}$. If the global test fails, local $w$-tests identify and iteratively exclude faulty satellites (up to 3 exclusions, minimum 5 remaining satellites). Second, the Horizontal Protection Level (HPL) is computed from the post-FDE covariance as $\text{HPL} = K\cdot \sigma_{\text{major}}$, where $\sigma_{\text{major}}$ is the semi-major axis of the error ellipse (Eq.~\eqref{eq:pl_iterative} simplified under single-fault RAIM). The HPL serves as the adaptive candidate search radius in CMM (Section~\ref{subsec:gnss_consistent_hmm}).

\subsubsection{Degradation Modes}

Two degradation modes are introduced to evaluate robustness:

\textbf{Cross-road occlusion.} To simulate building or tree blockage perpendicular to the road, satellites whose azimuth lies within $\pm 45^\circ$ of the cross-track direction and whose elevation is below $30^\circ$ are removed from the constellation. This elongates the positioning error ellipse in the cross-track direction, mimicking urban-corridor geometry degradation.

\textbf{Step fault injection.} For each trajectory, with probability $P_{\text{fault}}=0.3$, a randomly selected satellite receives a constant step bias $\Delta\rho \sim \mathcal{U}(100,500)$\,m on all its pseudorange observations throughout the entire trajectory. This simulates a slowly varying satellite clock or ephemeris fault that remains undetected at the measurement level and must be identified by the RAIM-FDE module.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Covariance Model Validation}
\label{sec:exp1}

The first experiment verifies that the GNSS covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ produced by the WLS solver (Eq.~\eqref{eq:wls_cov_full}) is statistically consistent with the actual positioning errors. If the covariance model is correct, the normalized innovation $\mathbf{d}_i = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1/2}(\mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{p}_i^{\text{true}})$ should follow a standard bivariate normal distribution, and the squared Mahalanobis distance $D_i^2 = \mathbf{d}_i^{\text{T}}\mathbf{d}_i$ should follow a $\chi^2(2)$ distribution.

For each $\sigma_{\rho}\in\{1,5,10,15,20,25,30\}$\,m, 20 trajectories (20,000 epochs total) are generated under nominal conditions (no occlusion, no fault). The per-epoch covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ is computed via the WLS solution using the known pseudorange noise model, and the positioning error $\mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{p}_i^{\text{true}}$ is obtained from the known ground truth. Four diagnostics are applied:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{$\chi^2(2)$ histogram:} The empirical distribution of $D_i^2$ is compared against the theoretical $\chi^2(2)$ density.
\item \textbf{P-P plot:} The empirical CDF of $D_i^2$ is plotted against the theoretical $\chi^2(2)$ CDF.
\item \textbf{Whitened error scatter:} The normalized errors $\mathbf{d}_i$ are plotted in the plane with $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ circles.
\item \textbf{Rayleigh CDF:} The radial error CDF is compared against the Rayleigh distribution predicted from the covariance.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Experiment 4: Wrong Emission Model --- Effect of Sigma Mismatch}
\label{sec:exp4}

In practice, the WLS solver's assumed pseudorange noise $\sigma_{\text{wls}}$ may differ from the true $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}}$, producing systematically biased covariance estimates. This experiment isolates the effect of such mismatch by fixing the WLS noise model at $\sigma_{\text{wls}}=20$\,m while varying the true pseudorange noise $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}} \in \{10,15,20,25,30\}$\,m. When $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}} < \sigma_{\text{wls}}$ (e.g., pr10\_wls20), the reported covariance is \emph{over-conservative}: the error ellipse is too large relative to the true error. When $\sigma_{\rho}^{\text{true}} > \sigma_{\text{wls}}$ (e.g., pr30\_wls20), the covariance is \emph{over-confident}: the ellipse is too small.

\subsection{Discussion of Simulation Findings}

Across all five experiments, three consistent patterns emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Covariance consistency is empirically verified.} Experiment~1 confirms that the WLS-derived $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ matches the true error distribution across all $\sigma_{\rho}$ levels (KS statistic $<$ 0.015 for CMM vs. 0.07--0.14 for the isotropic model), providing the statistical foundation for the Mahalanobis emission model.

\item \textbf{CMM outperforms FMM on both accuracy and calibration.} Experiments~3--5 collectively cover 7 noise levels, 4 sample rates, 5 mismatch conditions, and 4 degradation modes. In every configuration, CMM achieves higher segment accuracy and lower ECE than FMM. The advantage is most pronounced at moderate-to-high noise ($\sigma_{\rho} \ge 10$\,m) and under fault injection, where the anisotropic emission model provides the greatest discriminative benefit.

\item \textbf{Trustworthiness is a calibrated mismatch detector.} The joint path posterior probability (Eq.~\eqref{eq:joint_posterior}) achieves ROC AUC values between 0.66 and 0.88 for CMM across all experiments, confirming that it encodes meaningful reliability information. FMM's trustworthiness, by contrast, shows near-random discriminative power (AUC $<$ 0.6) at moderate-to-high noise levels, consistent with its poorly calibrated ECE.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Real Vehicle Experiment and Algorithm Validation}
\label{sec:real-experiment}

A real vehicle field test was conducted to validate the proposed covariance-aware 
HMM map-matching algorithm under practical operating conditions. The experiment 
was designed to assess matching accuracy, trustworthiness effectiveness for mismatch alert and computational feasibility.

\subsection{Dataset}
The real-vehicle evaluation uses 7 GNSS trajectories collected in Haikou,
Hainan Province, China (see Fig.~\ref{fig:dataset}). The data were recorded across
June 19 and 21, 2025 using a Tersus BX50C multi-GNSS receiver capable of
outputting raw RINEX 3.04 observations and standard NMEA GGA/GST messages,
the latter providing real-time estimates of horizontal positioning covariance.
The receiver provides GPS, BDS, GLONASS, Galileo, and QZSS observations at
10\,Hz.

Ground truth road labels were obtained via RTK post-processing (cm-level),
allowing direct per-epoch evaluation of whether each matched road segment
agrees with the true segment. The Protection Level for each epoch was computed
using a residual-based RAIM algorithm (PFA $= 10^{-5}$, PMD $= 10^{-3}$)
from the raw RINEX observations and broadcast ephemeris, replacing the
receiver-reported PL with a rigorous integrity bound. The RAIM HPL has a
median value of 22.8\,m across the 7 trajectories.

\subsection{Compared Methods}
The proposed CMM is benchmarked against the classical Fast Map Matching
(FMM)~\cite{FMM}, a widely-used HMM-based method that uses a fixed,
isotropic Gaussian emission model with an UBODT-precomputed candidate search.
FMM serves as the primary baseline: it uses the same road network, UBODT
table, and Viterbi decoder as CMM, differing only in the emission model
(isotropic Euclidean vs.\ anisotropic Mahalanobis). All evaluations use
the same RAIM-derived protection levels as input.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}
\textbf{1. Matching Accuracy.}
Matching accuracy is the fraction of epochs where the matched road segment
agrees with the RTK ground truth.

\textbf{2. Probabilistic Calibration.}
Beyond accuracy, we evaluate whether the softmax-smoothed posterior
(trustworthiness) is \emph{calibrated}—i.e., among all epochs assigned
a confidence $c$, a fraction $c$ should indeed be correct.
We report the Expected Calibration Error (ECE, 10-bin), Maximum
Calibration Error (MCE), Brier Score, and Log-Loss at error thresholds of
3\,m, 5\,m, and 10\,m. A well-calibrated model produces a reliability
diagram close to the diagonal $y=x$.

\textbf{3. Ablation Study.}
We quantify the per-component contribution to calibration by testing four
configurations in order of increasing complexity:
(1)~FMM baseline (isotropic emission);
(2)~CMM anisotropic Mahalanobis, lag~$=0$;
(3)~CMM with fixed-lag smoothing;
(4)~CMM with PHMI integrity monitoring.

\textbf{4. Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC).}

Because the proposed Trustworthiness metric outputs a continuous score rather than a hard binary label, we also utilize the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve to evaluate its discriminative power across different threshold settings. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) is used to summarize the overall mismatch detection performance.

\textbf{5. Trustworthiness Separation.}
We evaluate the ability of the trustworthiness score to discriminate between correct and wrong matches via the separation metric $\mu_{\text{correct}} - \mu_{\text{wrong}}$, where $\mu$ denotes the mean TW in each group. A larger separation indicates better discriminative power.

\subsection{ROC Analysis}
\label{sec:roc}

Fig.~\ref{fig:roc_curve} shows the ROC curves for CMM and FMM using
trustworthiness as a continuous mismatch detector. CMM attains an AUC of
0.764 across all 7 trajectories, while FMM achieves 0.965.
Superficially, FMM appears to have superior discriminative power, but
this is an artifact of TW compression: FMM assigns TW $\approx 1.0$ to
nearly every epoch regardless of correctness (standard deviation 0.039),
so the tiny spread between correct (0.996) and wrong (0.911) scores
produces an inflated AUC. The practical detection performance tells a
different story. At a threshold of 0.9, CMM rejects 43\% of wrong matches
while retaining 87\% of correct matches; FMM rejects only 26\% of wrong
matches at the same threshold. CMM's trustworthiness is therefore a more
\textit{actionable} integrity indicator: a drop in TW from 0.93 to 0.60
signals genuine ambiguity, whereas FMM's TW remains near 1.0 until the
mismatch is already severe.

The joint path posterior (Eq.~\eqref{eq:joint_posterior}) is inherently
more conservative than the marginal filtering posterior
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:filtering_posterior}), because it accumulates path
ambiguity across multiple epochs. This conservatism reduces AUC relative
to a trivially binary score, but provides the practical benefit of
meaningful confidence levels that correlate with correctness---precisely
the property required for safety-critical integrity monitoring.

\paragraph{Failure Case: Traj 22 Wrong-Direction False Lock.}
Traj~22 contains a systematic failure spanning epochs 1800--2052,
where CMM locks onto wrong-direction edges on the opposite side of a
dual carriageway. The Viterbi path correctly identifies the correct
forward edge at the individual-epoch level, but the global optimal
path transitions to the wrong edge (edge~76260) because the
subsequent GPS bias favors the incorrect geometry. CMM's
trustworthiness remains high ($>0.99$) during this period---a known
limitation of the filtering posterior, which considers only past
evidence and cannot retroactively correct an error once the Viterbi
path has switched. This case motivates future work incorporating
geometry-based anti-wrong-way guards and velocity-informed discount
factors.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper introduced a GNSS-uncertainty-consistent HMM framework
for map matching that propagates the full covariance matrix,
protection level, and integrity risk through all stages of the
matching pipeline. The key contributions are: (1)~an anisotropic
Mahalanobis emission model with probabilistically normalized HMM
components (background state, row-normalized transitions, uniform
prior) that improves calibration over the traditional isotropic
baseline (ECE reduced from 0.107 to 0.078); (2)~a joint path posterior
trustworthiness metric that quantifies the probability that the
optimal partial path is correct, together with a complementary
marginal filtering posterior (n\_best\_trustworthiness) for
per-candidate assessment; and (3)~a residual-based RAIM approach for
computing protection levels from raw GNSS observations, replacing
receiver-reported PL with a rigorous integrity bound (median HPL
22.8\,m).

On real-vehicle data across 7 trajectories, CMM achieves 91.4\%
segment accuracy (vs.\ 88.1\% for FMM), a 27\% relative ECE reduction,
and 3.9$\times$ better trustworthiness separation between correct and
wrong matches. The joint path posterior trustworthiness attains an AUC
of 0.764 for mismatch detection, providing a principled integrity
indicator without requiring a separately trained anomaly detector.
We further demonstrate that the high AUC of the FMM baseline (0.965)
is an artifact of near-binary TW scores that lack actionable
discriminative power, underscoring the importance of evaluating
trustworthiness separation alongside AUC.

Several limitations remain. Traj~22 exhibits a systematic wrong-direction
false lock that the current filtering posterior cannot correct, and the
background state with a fixed $p_{\text{bg}}=0.1$ does not fully prevent
overconfidence in all scenarios. Future work will incorporate a
geometry-based discount factor ($\alpha_{\text{geom}}$) and
velocity-informed discount factor ($\alpha_{\text{vel}}$) to address these
failure modes, extend the trustworthiness framework to multi-sensor fusion
(IMU dead-reckoning) for maintaining map-matching integrity in GNSS-denied
environments, and explore the delta-entropy metric as a complementary
reliability gauge alongside trustworthiness.
